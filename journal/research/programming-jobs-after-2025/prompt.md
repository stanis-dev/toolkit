## What has already been researched

- **Weak post-2025 demand baseline:** The strongest current signal is that software-development openings remained materially below pre-pandemic baselines through early 2026 in major advanced markets, even where the decline had stabilized somewhat.
- **Seniority polarization:** The evidence base consistently points to senior/staff and manager-level roles holding up better than junior and standard-level roles, with junior hiring the most pressured lane.
- **AI inside mainstream jobs, not mainly as a separate category:** Employers appear to be absorbing AI expectations into ordinary software, platform, systems, and data roles rather than creating a wholly separate "AI jobs" market.
- **Work rebundling:** The role shift looks less like straight replacement and more like cheaper code generation paired with more importance for system design, debugging, validation, testing, security, and architectural ownership.
- **Likely stronger lanes:** Senior/staff IC, backend/platform, SRE/infrastructure, and systems-heavy roles look relatively better positioned than junior and narrower implementation-heavy roles.
- **Personal relevance:** My own profile is closer to the senior/staff IC, backend/platform, AI-systems, and performance-optimization side of the market than to junior or management-heavy tracks.
- **Main unresolved questions:** How much of the compression is specifically AI-driven versus macro/post-boom correction, whether junior hiring recovers, and whether cheaper software creation eventually expands total demand enough to offset shrinking implementation-heavy work.

## Research focus

The core question: **How are mainstream programming jobs evolving after January 1, 2025 as AI reshapes software work, and what is the most defensible role-by-role trajectory after April 14, 2026?**

### 1. What has measurably changed in programming labor demand since January 1, 2025?

Investigate changes in openings, hiring velocity, layoffs and restructurings, contractor versus full-time demand, salary bands, and required experience levels. Look for role-specific rather than aggregate claims.

### 2. Which programming roles are being redefined rather than simply shrinking or growing?

Examine how core responsibilities, expected output, tooling, review practices, and scope are changing across role types and seniority bands.

### 3. Which roles or layers of the software org are showing the clearest signs of compression, expansion, or polarization?

Test whether the market is hollowing out particular tiers, increasing demand for other tiers, or splitting jobs into new subtypes.

### 4. How are hiring criteria changing for non-AI programming jobs?

Study shifts in interview loops, portfolio expectations, AI-tool fluency requirements, domain-expertise premiums, and the relative value of traditional credentials versus proof of leverage.

### 5. What do the strongest direct anecdotes from engineering leaders, recruiters, hiring managers, and practicing engineers say that the quantitative data does not yet fully capture?

Use only unusually concrete anecdotal evidence. Prefer firsthand statements tied to hiring decisions, org redesigns, staffing plans, or actual workflow changes.

### 6. What is the most defensible forward trajectory for the main programming job categories after April 14, 2026?

Provide role-by-role expectations for the next 12 to 36 months. Use scenarios, confidence levels, and leading indicators instead of deterministic forecasts.

### 7. What would falsify the current narrative?

Identify the key indicators that would prove the apparent trends are hype, cyclical noise, or temporary dislocation rather than structural change.

### 8. What do these shifts imply specifically for a hands-on senior/staff IC profile with backend, platform, AI-systems, infrastructure, and performance-optimization depth?

Identify which lanes appear most resilient, which ones are getting more competitive, which skills are becoming table stakes versus differentiators, and where this profile is most exposed to compression despite being on the stronger side of the market.
