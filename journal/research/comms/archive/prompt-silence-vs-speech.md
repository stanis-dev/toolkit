## Research focus: when to speak and when to remain silent

My existing research covers *how* to communicate (templates, scripts, altitude, framing) and *how* to project quiet confidence. What it does not cover is the **decision layer above that: when speaking is the right move and when silence is more powerful.**

This is a real tension in my environment:

- As a **remote contractor**, silence risks invisibility. Proximity bias research says out of sight = out of mind. If I don't speak, I may not be remembered, included, or credited.
- But as someone pursuing **quiet confidence**, silence is also a core tool. Speaking too much, too often, or at the wrong moment dilutes authority. The person who talks the least but says the most valuable thing carries disproportionate weight.
- My **ADHD** adds a third dimension: the impulse to contribute, to fill silence, to demonstrate value through volume -- which directly conflicts with the "speak less, mean more" principle.

I need a rigorous framework for deciding, in real time, **when to open my mouth and when to keep it shut** -- across meetings, Slack, standups, 1:1s, and group threads.

### Specific questions I want answered

1. **What does the research say about talk time and perceived competence/influence?** Is there a measurable relationship between how much someone speaks and how they're perceived? Does this change based on status (contractor vs employee), gender, seniority, or remote/in-person? I want actual findings, not folk wisdom.

2. **When does silence build authority and when does it signal disengagement?** What distinguishes "powerful silence" from "invisible silence"? Are there specific cues, contexts, or timing patterns that determine how silence is interpreted? How does this interact with Johnstone's status theory and Gruenfeld's strategic switching (already in my reference)?

3. **What is the optimal contribution pattern in meetings for someone in my position?** Not "speak up more" or "listen more" -- I want a model. When in a meeting's arc should I contribute? How does the ideal pattern differ between standups (customer-facing), technical syncs (peer-level), and broader group calls (where status dynamics are sharpest)?

4. **Silence as a negotiation and influence tool.** Voss, Ury, and Hughes are already in my reference. Extend this: what do these authors (or adjacent literature) say specifically about *strategic silence* as a technique? Silence after making a request. Silence after being challenged. Silence when someone expects you to fill the gap. What are the mechanics and when does each apply?

5. **Async silence: when to reply and when not to in Slack/written channels.** My environment is heavily async. There's a whole decision space around: should I respond to this thread? Should I add context here? Should I let this message sit? I want a decision model for written channel participation that accounts for contractor visibility needs and the risk of over-presence.

6. **The "last word" dynamic.** When is having the last word in a conversation powerful, and when does it signal insecurity? When should I let someone else close the thread, and when does silence after a challenge read as concession vs composure?

7. **How does ADHD impulse control interact with strategic silence?** I know the impulse to speak comes from ADHD patterns. I need more than "just hold back." I want specific cognitive techniques, environmental setups, or pre-commitment strategies that make it easier to choose silence when silence is right -- without the internal cost of constant suppression.

8. **Cultural dimension: UK communication norms around silence.** I work with a London office. British communication culture has its own relationship with silence, understatement, and indirectness. How does the "speak vs stay silent" calculus shift in a UK professional context compared to, say, a US startup norm?

### What I want as output

- A **decision model** for when to speak vs when to stay silent, applicable across my specific interaction types (standups, syncs, Slack, 1:1s, group calls)
- **Behavioral markers** that distinguish authority-building silence from invisibility-creating silence
- **Tactical scripts** for the moments around silence: how to enter silence, how to break it, how to signal engagement without speaking
