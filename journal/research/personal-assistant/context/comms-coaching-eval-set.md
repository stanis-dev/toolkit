# Communication Coaching Eval Set

Draft benchmark of authored async messages to evaluate the communication-coaching job against the quiet-confidence target defined in `/Users/stan/code/toolkit/journal/research/comms`.

## Scope

- 12 authored async messages
- Mix of strong, weak, and ambiguous examples
- Primary channels: Slack DM, Slack group thread, Teams direct chat, Teams meeting chat
- Purpose: derive a stable v1 coaching rubric and instruction block before any broader monitoring or automation

## Rubric

Scores are `1-5`, with `N/A` where a dimension does not apply.

- `5`: strong alignment
- `4`: good alignment, minor improvement possible
- `3`: mixed / situational
- `2`: weak alignment
- `1`: actively counterproductive

Dimensions:

1. **Uncertainty reduction**
2. **Response friction**
3. **Composure**
4. **Warmth-competence balance**
5. **Communication altitude**
6. **Constraint framing**
7. **Participation calibration**
8. **Authority growth**
9. **Penultimate-word discipline** for async threads

## Composition

- **Strong:** 6 items
- **Ambiguous:** 3 items
- **Weak:** 3 items

## Authority mechanism taxonomy

Each item is tagged with its **primary authority mechanism**:

- `Clarity anchor`
- `Bounded ask`
- `Ownership signal`
- `Decision framing`
- `Formulation`
- `Calibrated pushback`
- `Strategic visibility`
- `Trust-preserving warmth`
- `Authority maintenance`

## Immediate implications

- The clearest weak patterns are:
  - assuming before clarifying
  - bundling too many asks into one message
  - using apology/excuse language where a clean commitment would be enough
  - leaving the next move too underspecified
  - negotiating the urgency of your own ask downward before anyone else does
  - adding self-undermining commentary after otherwise competent work
  - explaining your communication style instead of just communicating clearly
- The clearest strong patterns are:
  - turning constraints into routing problems
  - replying with calm decisiveness under ambiguity
  - using low-drama clarity in sensitive situations
  - giving structured updates that preserve trust while naming uncertainty honestly
  - leading with state/action/risk before explanation
  - keeping warmth when it is low-cost and removing it when it weakens clarity
- The first coaching instruction block should therefore emphasize:
  - one ask / one outcome
  - clarify before inferring
  - no collapse-language unless repair is actually needed
  - when saying no, be clear early
  - when status-reporting, lead with the executive summary before details
  - do not pre-negotiate the importance of your own request downward
  - do not use self-deprecation as social lubricant in high-clarity contexts
  - default to the standard that would hold up in the hardest arena, then loosen only if the softer version remains equally clear
