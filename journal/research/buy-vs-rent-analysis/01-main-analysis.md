# Invest Now, Buy Later: Financial Plan

---

## 1. Strategy

Invest aggressively from day one in a 100% equity portfolio (55% World / 25% Europe / 20% EM). Rent cheaply during the
accumulation phase. When the portfolio reaches a sufficient percentage of the property's total cost, partially liquidate
via tax-free traspaso to fund a down payment, take a 65–75% LTV mortgage on a coastal flat in Castellón (Moncofa
primary, Xilxes/Nules backup), and invest the monthly surplus above minimum mortgage payments. After mortgage payoff,
redirect full capacity to retirement accumulation.

The full comparison analysis (buy now vs rent forever vs invest-then-buy) is preserved in
[alternatives/](alternatives/).

## 5. Purchase Timeline and Trigger Framework

### Partial mortgage mechanics

Liquidating the entire portfolio to buy outright triggers CGT on all embedded gains at once, pushing into the 23–30%
brackets. A 65–75% LTV mortgage at purchase avoids this: only ~37–47% of the portfolio is liquidated (keeping gains in
the 19–21% brackets), while the retained portion continues compounding tax-deferred via traspaso. The mortgage interest
(~2.5–3.5%) is far cheaper than the CGT avoided. Breakeven portfolio return is only ~1–3%.

### Purchase trigger

The optimal time to buy depends on the **spread between portfolio return and property appreciation**, observable by year
5–6.

| Observed spread (return − appreciation) | Trigger (% of total cost) |  LTV   | Indicative age |
| --------------------------------------- | :-----------------------: | :----: | :------------: |
| Portfolio ≈ property (spread <1pp)      |        **60–75%**         | 70–80% |     41–44      |
| Portfolio ahead by ~1.5pp               |        **70–90%**         | 65–75% |     43–48      |
| Portfolio ahead by 2+pp                 |       **100–120%**        | 65–75% |     48–52      |

100% coverage is never the exact optimum — either buy earlier (when property keeps pace) or later (when portfolio
outpaces property). The spread is the deciding variable.

### Purchase execution protocol

When the trigger is reached:

1. **Traspaso only the gross house-purchase amount** (withdrawal + CGT + buffer) from equity into the money market fund
   (tax-free). The rest of the portfolio stays in equities — do not de-risk the retirement engine for the house.
2. **December/January split**: redeem the first tranche from the money market fund in late December (taxed in current
   year), second tranche in early January (taxed in following year). Saves €100–1,100 depending on total gains (higher
   gains = more bracket reuse = more savings).
3. **Take a 65–75% LTV fixed-rate mortgage** on the property. Target the best bonified rate available (currently
   ~2.1–2.5% TIN with bundling).
4. **Make minimum mortgage payments.** At the 70–80% trigger on a €200K target (~€183–188K mortgage at ~3.0% over 20yr),
   minimum payments are ~€1,016–1,045/mo. **Invest the ~€455–484/mo surplus** in equity fondos. At current rates
   (~2.5–3.5% mortgage vs ~5–6% expected equity return), investing the surplus produces ~3–4x more expected wealth than
   overpaying. Switch to aggressive overpayment only if the mortgage rate exceeds ~5%. See baseline table for all
   trigger levels.
5. **Retain €60–100K+ in the portfolio** (depending on trigger level). Traspaso the retained equity back to the growth
   core (100% equity). This continues compounding tax-deferred for 20+ years toward retirement.
6. **After mortgage payoff**, redirect full €1,500/mo to retirement DCA.

### Mortgage phase

The default is **minimum payments + invest the surplus** (see baseline table for payment/surplus figures by trigger
level). At current Spanish fixed rates (~2.5–3.5% TIN), the expected equity return (~5–6% nominal) exceeds the mortgage
rate, making the surplus more productive in equity fondos than in accelerated payoff.

The partial mortgage advantage does not depend on what you do with the surplus:

- **CGT saving (~€10–20K)** is locked in at purchase regardless of repayment speed
- **Retained portfolio (~€60–100K)** compounds regardless of repayment speed
- **Breakeven portfolio return remains ~2–3%** whether you overpay or not

**Switch to aggressive repayment if:**

- Mortgage rate exceeds ~5% (the conservative threshold where overpayment beats the portfolio's expected after-tax
  return over 10–15 years; at current Spanish rates of ~2.5–3.5%, investing the surplus is clearly favourable)
- Income drops and the surplus becomes unreliable (overpaying reduces fixed obligations faster)
- Preference for simplicity: if reducing the number of concurrent financial obligations is a priority, overpaying is a
  reasonable practical choice

The mortgage remains structurally cheaper than the CGT you avoided. The surplus question is an optimization within an
already-optimal structure.

### Fragility floor

Never trigger below **60% portfolio coverage**. Below that, a -30% equity crash post-purchase leaves insufficient
buffer. The minimum safe coverage by LTV:

- 60% LTV: minimum 50% coverage
- 70% LTV: minimum 60% coverage
- 80% LTV: minimum 70% coverage

IVF guarantee programme covers only the tranche above 80% LTV — irrelevant at the planned 65–75% LTV. Retained as a
contingency if circumstances require >80% LTV before age 45.

## 6. Rental Stability During Investment Phase

Securing stable, affordable housing for ~10–15 years is critical. Spanish rental law (LAU) provides strong protections
for _vivienda habitual_ leases.

### Contract stability under LAU

| Landlord type | Mandatory minimum | Tacit renewal | Total potential stay |
| ------------- | ----------------- | ------------- | -------------------- |
| Individual    | **5 years**       | + up to 3yr   | **~8 years**         |
| Legal entity  | **7 years**       | + up to 3yr   | **~10 years**        |

- Landlord must give **4 months' notice** to not renew; tenant gives **2 months'**
- Sale of the property does not break the lease during the protected period (Art. 14 LAU)

### In-contract rent updates

- Only once per year, only if the contract has an update clause (Art. 18 LAU)
- Limited by the INE reference index (2023 Housing Law)
- In practice: modest, index-like increases (~2–3%/yr)

### Market resets between contracts

- When a contract ends, rent resets to market rate
- "Stressed area" caps do not apply in Castellón (no municipalities declared)
- This is the main rent risk: periodic jumps of 15–20% at contract boundaries

### Rent inflation scenarios

| Scenario          | Avg annual |  Year 10 |  Year 15 |  Year 20 |
| ----------------- | ---------: | -------: | -------: | -------: |
| Low (1.5%)        |       1.5% |     €580 |     €625 |     €673 |
| **Baseline (3%)** |     **3%** | **€672** | **€779** | **€903** |
| High (6%)         |         6% |     €895 |   €1,198 |   €1,604 |

### Coastal-specific risk

On the Castellón coast, landlords may prefer **seasonal contracts** (_arrendamientos por temporada_), which do not carry
LAU stability protections. **Only sign _vivienda habitual_ contracts**, even if the selection is smaller.

## 7. Risk Assessment

### Equity market crashes

| Scenario                                | Impact on smooth portfolio   | Notes                                         |
| --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| -40% crash at year 2, recovery over 5yr | −11% to −18% of final value  | Survivable: 10+ years of DCA buys cheap units |
| -40% crash at year 10 (near purchase)   | Delays purchase by 2–4 years | Flexibility to wait is the key advantage      |

An early crash during the accumulation phase is manageable — continued monthly contributions buy more units at depressed
prices. A crash _near_ the intended purchase date means waiting longer, not losing money (the portfolio recovers, you
just delay).

### Property crash: helps the buy-later strategy

A property crash during the investment phase makes the target **cheaper**. If Castellón has another -20% to -40% crash,
the purchase can happen sooner and at a lower price. With no mortgage during accumulation, there is no negative equity
risk.

### Property appreciates faster than portfolio

The worst-case scenario for this strategy: property grows at 4%/yr while investment returns are only 5% (the pessimistic
scenario). At those assumptions, the portfolio still grows faster than the property — but the margin is thin and the
timeline extends significantly. For the €200K target at 5% return and 4% appreciation, purchase is reachable but at a
later age than the baseline table indicates.

**Mitigation:** increase DCA capacity, target a lower price point within Moncofa/Xilxes/Nules, or accept a longer
timeline. The strategy remains viable at pessimistic returns if property appreciation is moderate (2.5% baseline).

### Income instability

A mortgage is a rigid multi-decade obligation. This portfolio is a liquid asset with zero ongoing obligation. If income
is disrupted:

- **Buy-later investor:** pause DCA instantly, draw from portfolio if needed, move to a cheaper flat. A delay is just a
  delay — not a crisis.
- **Immediate mortgage buyer (for comparison):** would owe ~€1,449/mo at 95% LTV on a €250K flat, needing €4,071/mo net
  to stay below 35% housing ratio, with €56–95K net losses if forced to sell early.

Income disruption risk favours the invest-then-buy approach: a delay is a delay, not a crisis.

## 8. Tax Optimization Roadmap

### Capital gains tax (rendimientos del ahorro) — 2025+ rates

| Gain bracket      | Combined rate |
| ----------------- | ------------: |
| First €6,000      |           19% |
| €6,000–€50,000    |           21% |
| €50,000–€200,000  |           23% |
| €200,000–€300,000 |           27% |
| Above €300,000    |           30% |

### Traspaso-based rebalancing

Execute all allocation shifts (geographic rebalancing, equity → money market before purchase, provider switches) via
traspasos. No taxable event until final sale. This is the single most important tax-efficiency lever.

### CGT at flat purchase (partial liquidation strategy)

At purchase, ~37–47% of the portfolio is liquidated (depending on trigger level); the rest is retained. The partial
approach keeps most gains in the 19–21% CGT brackets.

**Execution:** traspaso equity to money market (tax-free), then redeem in a December/January split across two tax years.
This reuses the 19% and 21% brackets twice, saving €100–1,100 per additional tax year (depending on total gains).

At the conservative scenario (€1.5K/mo, 5% return, 70–80% trigger on €200K target — see baseline table):

- Portfolio: ~€189–222K. Liquidation: ~€89–93K gross. Retained: ~€100–129K.
- CGT on liquidated portion: ~€4–6K. Effective rate: ~20–21%
- Budget a ~€1–2K post-sale tax top-up reserve (withholding is 19%, actual may be 21%)

### Planes de pensiones (small supplement)

Contribute up to €1,500/year if your current marginal IRPF rate is meaningfully higher than your expected retirement
rate. The annual tax saving is modest (€90–200/yr depending on marginal rate) but compounds over time. Accept the
illiquidity: these funds are locked until retirement (with a 10-year liquidity rule from 2025 for qualifying
contributions).

### Wealth tax monitoring

Valencian mínimo exento: €1,000,000 net taxable wealth. Monitor as the portfolio grows. Primary residence has a partial
exemption (up to €300K) — once you buy the flat, this shelters part of your wealth from the tax.

### FIFO management

Spain uses first-in-first-out for cost basis. The oldest (most appreciated) lots are sold first. Keep records. When
eventually selling to buy the flat, this is unavoidable — but after purchase, new contributions start fresh cost bases.

## Investment Policy Statement

Operating rules for the full accumulation phase. The system is designed for **consistency over decades**: clear
structures, automated execution, and defined optimization boundaries. Drawdowns of 30–50% are expected events over a
30-year equity-only horizon — the system is built to continue operating through them, not to avoid them. Optimization
within defined rules is encouraged. Ad-hoc changes outside the review cycle are not.

### The system

1. **DCA is automated on payday.** Set up as standing orders (three funds — World/Europe/EM), not manual monthly
   actions. Base amounts are set at the start of each year.

2. **Quarterly contribution review.** Every 3 months, check the geographic allocation balance (~55/25/20
   World/Europe/EM) and adjust the next quarter's contribution split to steer back toward target. This is structured
   micro-optimization to maintain target allocation. Use contribution direction only — no traspasos unless any region
   drifts beyond 10pp of target after a full quarter of directed contributions.

3. **During a drawdown: the system continues unchanged.** A 100% equity portfolio will experience 30–50% drawdowns —
   this is the expected cost of capturing the equity premium over 30+ years. The standing orders keep running. A 6-month
   DCA pause during a 30% drawdown costs ~€15–20K in missed recovery contributions. If spare cash is available during a
   drawdown, directing it to the most-depressed regional fund is a valid optimization.

4. **Annual strategic review.** Once per year, check: fund TERs, tax paperwork, platform status, rent contract, buffer
   adequacy, return-vs-appreciation spread (from year 5), and whether the multi-ISIN split needs adjusting. This is the
   only time to make structural changes (add/remove a fund, change platform, revise the target).

5. **Optimize within the system, never outside it.** Valid optimizations at any time:
   - Adjusting contribution split between funds (quarterly)
   - Directing bonus/windfall cash to the underweight sleeve
   - Researching tax or platform improvements for the next annual review

   Forbidden without an annual review decision:
   - Switching funds or adding new asset classes
   - Changing allocation targets
   - Acting on market news, predictions, or "feelings"
   - Pausing or reducing automated contributions

6. **Three emergency overrides:**
   - Job loss exceeding 3 months (pause DCA, draw from buffer, resume when employed)
   - Medical emergency requiring portfolio access
   - Platform failure requiring immediate traspaso to MyInvestor

### FIFO optimization through geographic diversification

The 3-fund regional approach (World/Europe/EM) naturally creates **three separate FIFO queues** with distinct return
paths. At purchase, redeem from the fund with the lowest embedded gains — this minimizes the taxable event. No
same-index clones needed; geographic diversification provides both allocation benefit and tax optionality.

**Implementation** (see Section 3 fund table for ISINs and TERs):

- At purchase, redeem from the fund with lowest embedded gains first. Retain the higher-gain funds for continued
  tax-deferred compounding toward retirement.
- Optional: from year 3–4 onward, switch the World sleeve to a different ISIN (e.g., Vanguard Global Stock,
  IE00B03HD191) to create a fourth queue with a younger cost basis. Review at annual check.

## 10. Alternative Strategies Considered

This plan was chosen after a comprehensive three-way comparison of buy now (mortgage), rent forever (invest), and invest
now (buy later). The full analysis — including mortgage amortisation schedules, forced-sale risk tables, 3×3 comparison
matrices across property growth and investment return scenarios, sensitivity testing, equity/property crash stress
tests, and retirement cashflow modelling — is preserved in the reference documents below.

**Key finding from the comparison:** at a €500/mo rent baseline, renting + investing wins in 8/9 scenario combinations
at the end of a ~32-year timeline. Buying now with a mortgage wins only at optimistic property growth (4%) + pessimistic
investment returns (5%). The buy-later strategy captures the renter's compounding advantage while adding eventual
mortgage-free ownership.
