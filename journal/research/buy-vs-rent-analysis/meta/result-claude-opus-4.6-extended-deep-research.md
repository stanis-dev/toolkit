# Adversarial audit of a "Buy Later, Invest Now" strategy for Castellón coast

**The strategy is structurally sound but systematically biased toward optimism on three parameters that compound against you.** The 6% return assumption sits at the 60th–70th percentile of institutional forecasts, not the median; the €500/month rent assumption erodes sharply at year 8 when LAU protections expire; and the platform choice (Trade Republic for fondos) introduces unnecessary operational risk when a cheaper, more mature alternative exists. Together, these biases could shift the purchase timeline from age 48–51 to age 52–57 under plausible-but-not-extreme conditions. No single assumption is indefensible, but all lean the same direction — which means the combined probability of a materially delayed purchase is higher than any individual scenario suggests.

---

## 1. Property prices are confirmed but the asking-transaction gap conceals divergence

The February 2026 asking prices cited in the strategy are **exactly confirmed**: Benicàssim €2,796/m², Oropesa del Mar €2,004/m², Vinaròs ~€1,687/m² (Idealista via Valencia Extra, 4 March 2026). However, full-year 2025 notarial transaction data from the Consejo General del Notariado reveals a critical gap between asking and closing prices that varies dramatically by municipality.

| Municipality | Asking (Feb 2026) | Transaction (2025 avg) | Gap | YoY Transaction Growth |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Benicàssim | €2,796/m² | €2,624/m² | 6% | **+4.4%** |
| Oropesa del Mar | €2,004/m² | €1,673/m² | **20%** | +11.3% |
| Peñíscola | €2,204/m² | €1,907/m² | 16% | +18.3% |
| Vinaròs | ~€1,700/m² | €1,157/m² | **32%** | **−3.7%** |

**The Vinaròs anomaly is the most important finding.** Transaction prices fell 3.7% in 2025 despite asking prices rising ~10%. This means the cheapest target municipality — the one that makes the €200K target most accessible — has a softer market than headline numbers suggest. The asking-transaction gap of 32% in Vinaròs versus 6% in Benicàssim indicates that sellers in budget municipalities are pricing aspirationally while actual buyers are pushing back.

The claim that Castellón's 10-year CAGR is ~2.5% versus Spain's ~4% is **directionally correct but slightly understated for both**: Tinsa data shows Castellón province remains **31.7% below its 2007/2008 peak**, versus Valencia city at +9.2% above peak. A more precise characterization is **Castellón ~2.5–3.0% versus Spain ~4.5–5.0%**. The structural reasons — deeper post-2008 crash, lower employment dynamism, more available stock per capita — remain intact and are not cyclical.

**Assessment of the 1%/2.5%/4% scenarios:** The 2.5% base case is defensible for Castellón province overall. The 4% bull case is defensible for premium coastal municipalities (Benicàssim) in the near term but not for Vinaròs, where real transaction data shows stagnation. The 1% bear case requires an external shock but cannot be dismissed. Critically, the strategy should use **transaction prices, not asking prices**, for calibration — which makes target properties roughly 15–25% cheaper than asking-price models suggest.

**Bias direction:** The original assumption is approximately correct at the provincial level, but the strategy's reliance on asking prices for target-setting likely **overestimates** the purchase cost by €20K–€50K depending on municipality. This is one of the few biases that works in the strategy's favor.

Banco de España identifies Comunitat Valenciana as one of Spain's most supply-constrained regions (new housing covers only **25–30% of new household formation** in CV), but no Castellón municipality is declared a zona tensionada, and the current PP-led Generalitat has frozen the declaration process initiated in 2023.

## 2. The 6% return baseline is optimistic by roughly 100 basis points

The institutional consensus for 10-year forward nominal returns on MSCI World in EUR clusters around **4.5–5.5%**, not 6%. The assumption is not unreasonable, but it sits at the optimistic end of the range.

Current MSCI World valuations are stretched: the **trailing P/E is 24.3** versus a 20-year average of 15.8, and the **S&P 500 Shiller CAPE is 38.1** (96th percentile historically, median 16.0). Global equities CAPE stands at 27.7. Every major institution projects lower forward returns than the trailing 10-year period delivered.

| Institution | US Equities (10yr) | Non-US DM (10yr) | Implied MSCI World (USD) | Date |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vanguard VCMM | 3.5–5.5% | 4.9–6.9% | ~4.5–6.0% | Dec 2025 |
| J.P. Morgan LTCMA | 6.7% | 7.5% | ~7.0% | Sep 2025 |
| BlackRock | 5.2% | 7.1% | ~5.8% | Sep 2025 |
| Research Affiliates | 3.1% | 7.7% | ~4.5% | Dec 2025 |
| GMO (7yr) | −3.8% nominal | 1.5% nominal | ~−2.2% | Nov 2025 |

**The blended MSCI World estimate in USD is ~5.5–6.0%.** But the strategy runs in EUR, and MSCI World is ~70% USD-denominated. With the EUR/USD at ~1.16 and consensus forecasts pointing toward 1.20–1.25 by end-2026, the **currency drag is roughly 0.5–1.0% annually**, reducing the EUR-denominated estimate to approximately **4.5–5.5%**. The historical figure of 6.32% annualized since 2000 in EUR is correct, but that 25-year period started from deeply depressed post-dot-com valuations — the opposite of today's starting point.

**The verified figure:** MSCI World net return in EUR since December 31, 2000 was approximately **6.0–6.3% annualized** — confirmed by MSCI factsheets and multiple secondary sources. The Fidelity MSCI World Index Fund P-Acc-EUR (IE00BYX5NX33) shows a tracking difference of approximately **−0.10% per year**, slightly better than its 0.12% TER due to securities lending income.

**Impact of being wrong:** If actual returns are 5% rather than 6%, the €200K purchase target shifts from age ~48 to age ~50 (a 2-year delay). If returns are 4% while property grows at 3.5%, the delay extends to age ~52 for the €200K target and age ~57 for the €250K target. A 5% central assumption rather than 6% is the appropriate planning figure.

**Is 4% a reasonable pessimistic floor?** Yes, but it is not the worst case. The true 10th-percentile outcome over 10–15 years could be 2–3% nominal in EUR, driven by US valuation compression (CAPE 38→25) combined with meaningful EUR appreciation. Under this scenario, the purchase timeline extends to age 57–61.

## 3. The €500 rent assumption fractures at year 8

This is the most fragile parameter in the entire strategy. The Castellón coastal rental market is **structurally hostile to long-term habitual tenants**: an estimated **70–85% of rental listings are seasonal** (September-to-June, for teachers and temporary workers), not year-round. Rental stock in Castellón province has **shrunk 33% over six years**, and rents are rising at **10–12% annually**.

A genuine €500/month year-round rental is barely achievable today. Vinaròs is the only target municipality with a functioning habitual rental market — and even there, typical 2-bedroom apartments range from €500–€700. Benicàssim is overwhelmingly seasonal at €550–€850 for the rare habitual listing. **Peñíscola has essentially zero year-round habitual rental supply.**

The LAU (Ley de Arrendamientos Urbanos) protects tenants for a **5-year mandatory minimum** (individual landlords) with **3 years of automatic extensions**, creating an 8-year protected window. Annual increases during this period are capped by the IRAV index (currently ~2.1–2.3%). A €500 contract signed today would reach approximately **€583 by year 8** — manageable.

**The break point is year 9.** When the initial 5+3 year cycle expires, the landlord can terminate or renegotiate at market rates. Based on current rental inflation, a €500 apartment today would command **€650–800/month by 2034**. The realistic 15-year rental cost is **€110,000–€130,000**, not the €90,000 implied by a flat €500 assumption. That €20,000–€40,000 difference directly reduces money available for investment.

If rent escalates to €700/month after year 8, the monthly DCA capacity drops by €200 (assuming total budget is fixed), reducing monthly contributions from €1,500 to €1,300. This alone delays the purchase target by approximately **1–1.5 years**.

**Municipality ranking for long-term rental feasibility:** Vinaròs (best — real working town), Castellón de la Plana (large pool but not coastal), Oropesa pueblo (some options), Benicàssim (very difficult), Peñíscola (essentially impossible).

## 4. Tax assumptions mostly hold, with two material corrections

The tax verification confirms most original assumptions but reveals two changes worth noting and one important eligibility gap.

**Confirmed unchanged:** CGT brackets (19/21/23/27/30%, per Ley 7/2024). Traspaso tax-free regime for qualifying fondos de inversión (Art. 94 LIRPF, no proposed changes). FIFO cost basis on fund redemptions. Planes de pensiones €1,500/year individual limit. Modelo 720 foreign asset reporting threshold at €50,000 per category.

**Correction 1 — Wealth tax mínimo exento has DOUBLED.** Under Ley 5/2025 (Comunitat Valenciana), the exempt threshold increased from €500,000 to **€1,000,000**, effective for patrimonio assessed at 31 December 2025. The original question stated "€1M" — this is now correct, but was recently changed. The ITSGF (solidarity tax for large fortunes) is now **permanent** (extended indefinitely by RDL 8/2023) but effectively neutralized for Valencian residents since the regional wealth tax cuota absorbs the ITSGF liability.

**Correction 2 — IVF guarantee programme enhanced.** The IVF first-home guarantee now covers up to **100% of the lesser of valuation or purchase price** (up from 95% in 2025), with the maximum property price raised to **€311,000**. The programme budget increased to €30M for 2026. This is relevant as an emergency exit strategy: if the "buy later" plan needs to pivot to mortgage financing, the IVF guarantee eliminates the need for any down payment (though taxes and fees still apply).

**Eligibility gap:** The reduced ITP rates for young buyers (6% on properties ≤€180K, 8% above) require being **under 35 years old**. At 36, the person is ineligible. The applicable rate is the general **9%** (effective 1 June 2026 under Ley 5/2025, confirmed by Cuatrecasas and Garrigues). The AJD (Actos Jurídicos Documentados) drops from 1.5% to **1.4%** on the same date.

**Total acquisition costs at purchase:** 9% ITP + ~1% notary/registry/gestoría = approximately **10% of purchase price**. On a €200K property, this adds €20,000 to the total required.

## 5. Trade Republic is the wrong platform for this strategy

This is the most actionable finding in the audit. Trade Republic launched fondos de inversión in Spain on **5 June 2025** — only 9 months before this audit. The platform has **documented traspaso failures**: on the Bogleheads España forum, a user attempting a fund transfer to MyInvestor encountered CIF mismatches, non-existent transfer paths in the app, and ultimately **sold the position (triggering a taxable event) after giving up**. A CNMV complaint was filed and resolved in the user's favor.

Meanwhile, **MyInvestor has secured an exclusive arrangement with BlackRock** for iShares S-class funds — the cheapest index fund share classes available to retail investors in Spain. The iShares Developed World Index Fund S class (IE000ZYRH0Q7) has a TER of **0.06%**, half the cost of the Fidelity fund at 0.12%. Over a 15-year accumulation with a terminal portfolio of ~€300K, the 0.06% annual cost difference compounds to approximately **€2,500–€3,500 in saved fees** — modest but free money.

Duration risk (~7 years) is the main concern — a short-term bond alternative would reduce volatility in the defensive sleeve.

## 6. Timeline sensitivity shows asymmetric downside

The original model estimates purchase of a €200K flat at age ~48 and €250K at age ~51. My independent calculations confirm these figures under baseline assumptions (6% return, 2.5% property growth, 10% transaction costs).

| Scenario | Return | Property Growth | €200K Target | €250K Target | Delay vs Baseline |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Baseline** | 6% | 2.5% | Age 48 | Age 50–51 | — |
| **Adjusted baseline** | 5% | 2.5% | Age 50 | Age 52 | +2 years |
| **Pessimistic** | 4% | 3.5% | Age 52 | Age 57 | +4 / +6 years |
| **Severe** | 2% | 4% | Age 58 | Age 61+ | +10+ years |

The critical insight is **asymmetry**: the scenarios where things go better than expected compress the timeline by only 1–2 years (diminishing returns on higher investment returns), while scenarios where things go worse extend it by 4–10+ years. The median outcome, weighting institutional forecasts, is closer to age 50–52 for the €200K target than age 48.

**The "permanent renter" threshold** occurs when investment returns roughly equal property growth rates while both are high in nominal terms. At 3% returns with 4% property growth, contributions alone cannot close the gap fast enough — the €200K target doesn't hit until age ~61, and €250K becomes effectively unreachable before retirement. The probability of this scenario is roughly **10–15%** based on the spread of institutional forecasts.

**DCA adjustments that compensate:** Increasing monthly contributions from €1,500 to €1,800 (+€300) offsets approximately 1% of return underperformance, compressing the pessimistic timeline by ~2 years. If rent increases force a DCA reduction to €1,300, the combined effect (lower DCA + lower assumed returns) pushes the €250K target to age 58–60.

## 7. Three critically underweighted risks

**Climate risk is the largest unpriced exposure.** PATRICOVA flood risk mapping is currently under revision post-2024 DANA, and the Consell has confirmed the revision will **expand inundable surface areas** beyond current designations, with a new hazard level exceeding 500-year return periods. Specific damage claims from recent DANA events: Benicàssim €11.1M, Peñíscola €8.5M, Vinaròs €9.5M (after a 159 l/m²-in-one-hour event in 2018). The Consorcio de Compensación de Seguros paid over **€4 billion** after the October 2024 Valencia DANA — the most expensive insured event in Spanish history. Insurance repricing is underway across Comunitat Valenciana. There is **no evidence yet of climate risk being priced into Castellón coastal property values**, which means the repricing is ahead, not behind. When the expanded PATRICOVA maps are published, specific parcels could see sharp, localized value declines.

**Castellón province is projected to lose population.** INE projections through 2035 place Castellón among approximately 30 Spanish provinces with negative population growth, while Comunitat Valenciana's overall mild growth concentrates in Valencia city and Alicante. Foreign buyers represent **27–31% of transactions** in coastal municipalities (Oropesa 27%, Peñíscola 31%), making these markets dependent on external demand that can evaporate during geopolitical disruptions. The current Iran war, referenced in the ECB's 19 March 2026 statement, illustrates exactly this type of risk.

**ADHD behavioral risk is the highest-probability failure mode.** The strategy requires sustained automated DCA over 10–15 years, surviving at least 2–3 major drawdowns (20%+ declines). ADHD creates specific, documented neurological barriers: impulsive spending as dopamine reward, executive function deficits in sustained planning, time blindness that devalues long-term goals, and emotional dysregulation during financial stress. Automation mitigates but does not eliminate this — the person must not cancel or reduce the automation during drawdowns. Over a 15-year horizon, ADHD significantly increases the probability of DCA interruption during a market crash, which would be the single most damaging behavioral error. This risk should be treated as a near-certainty for at least one episode and the plan should include explicit protocols for it.

Additional risks that deserve monitoring: EUR/USD volatility (a 10% EUR appreciation equals ~7% drag on MSCI World EUR returns in that year); persistent inflation above ECB's 2% target (Spain core CPI at 2.7% in February 2026); and the theoretical risk of traspaso regime abolition (currently low probability, deeply embedded in Spanish investment culture, but the top savings CGT bracket was already raised from 28% to 30%).

## Implementation risks ranked by probability of mid-course correction

1. **Rental cost exceeds budget after year 8** — Probability: **75–85%**. The combination of a shrinking rental market (−33% stock in 6 years), rising rents (+10–12%/yr), and LAU protections expiring at year 8 makes a market-rate reset to €650–700+ near-certain. Timeline impact: **+1–2 years** if DCA is reduced.

2. **Investment returns underperform 6% baseline** — Probability: **60–70%** (based on institutional consensus centering around 5%). Timeline impact: **+1.5–3 years** for each 1% of underperformance.

3. **DCA discipline interrupted during a major drawdown** — Probability: **40–60%** over 15 years, elevated by ADHD. A 6-month DCA pause during a 30% drawdown would cost approximately €15K–€20K in missed recovery contributions, extending timeline by **~1 year**.

4. **Climate repricing of target property** — Probability: **20–35%** within the horizon. The PATRICOVA revision will expand flood zones; if a target property falls within a newly designated zone, insurance costs could rise 30–50% and resale value could drop 10–15%.

5. **Trade Republic traspaso failure at critical moment** — Probability: **25–40%** if the platform is used for the full accumulation. Operational risk of a forced taxable event (selling rather than transferring) could cost **€5K–€15K in accelerated CGT** depending on unrealized gains at the time.

## Conclusion: a sound strategy with knowable fragilities

The core logic of "Buy Later, Invest Now" is robust — it correctly exploits Spain's traspaso tax deferral, avoids mortgage interest costs, and harnesses compound returns during the accumulation phase. None of the seven axes of this audit reveals a fatal flaw. But the aggregate bias across all assumptions leans optimistic by roughly **2–3 years on the purchase timeline**. The most likely outcome, using adjusted-consensus returns (~5% nominal EUR) and realistic rental escalation, is a €200K purchase at **age 50–52** and a €250K purchase at **age 53–55**, not age 48–51. The two highest-impact pre-execution changes are switching from Trade Republic to MyInvestor for fondos (eliminating traspaso risk and halving fund costs) and budgeting for rental escalation from year 9. The hardest risk to mitigate — and the most likely failure mode — is not market risk, tax risk, or property risk. It is the behavioral risk of maintaining uninterrupted DCA through the inevitable drawdowns, amplified by ADHD. Automating everything, writing explicit protocols, and making the process as boring as possible is not just good practice — it is the load-bearing wall of the entire strategy.
