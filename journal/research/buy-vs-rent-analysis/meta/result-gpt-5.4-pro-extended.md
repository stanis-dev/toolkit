## Executive assessment

**Conditional go.** The implementation stack is still broadly viable: the Spanish **fondo de inversión + traspaso** tax wrapper remains intact, the Valencian **ITP cut to 9% from 1 June 2026** is real, and the chosen Fidelity fund is still a valid low-cost Spain-registered core holding. But the plan is **not robust enough to execute unchanged**. The weak points are not tax or TER; they are the **6% nominal central return**, the assumption that **€500/mo coastal rent** can be maintained for 10–15 years on a proper **vivienda habitual** lease, and the fact that “**Castellón coast €200k–€250k**” is too broad because **Benicàssim/Peñíscola** and **Oropesa/Vinaròs** are now behaving like different markets. Rebased to a more realistic working case—about **5% nominal portfolio return**, **municipality-specific property growth**, and an explicit **rent-stress + late-stage de-risking rule**—I would execute. Unchanged, your age-48/51 purchase expectation is too optimistic by roughly **2–7 years** depending on town and rent path. ([www3.agenciatributaria.gob.es][1])

## Assumption audit

### 1) Property-price assumptions: 1% / 2.5% / 4%

Current data say the **near-term market is hotter than your long-run scenario framing**. Official transaction-based indicators are still running very strong into early 2026: Spain’s **INE house-price index was +12.9% YoY in Q4 2025**, Registradores’ repeat-sales index was **+16.6% YoY**, and Tinsa put **Castellón province at +13.6% YoY** in Q4 2025. Municipality-level **asking** data for **February 2026** are also hot: **Benicàssim €2,796/m² (+19.1% YoY)**, **Peñíscola €2,204 (+16.9%)**, **Oropesa €2,004 (+14.5%)**, and **Vinaròs €1,683**. Benicàssim and Peñíscola are at or near historical highs, while Oropesa and Vinaròs still sit below prior peaks, which tells me Castellón is **not one market** right now. ([INE][2])

My judgment: **1% / 2.5% / 4% is still usable as a long-run province-wide nominal scenario set**, but it is **too blunt** for implementation. If you target **Benicàssim or Peñíscola**, a working central case closer to **3%–4% nominal** is more defensible today; if you stay flexible and are genuinely open to **Oropesa or Vinaròs**, **2%–3%** still looks reasonable. That means your original **2.5% baseline is biased low for the premium towns** and roughly fair for the cheaper towns. I also could **not** verify from current official sources the earlier claim that Castellón is formally “outside the Banco de España bottleneck list”; BdE still describes the deficit as concentrated in more dynamic economic and tourist areas, but I did not find a current province ranking I would rely on. ([bde.es][3])

Quantified impact: keeping **€1,500/mo DCA**, if you shift the working assumption from **5% returns / 3% property growth** to **5% / 4%**, my rerun moves the **€250k cash-buy date from age ~53.1 to ~58.0**. For a **€200k** target, it moves from **~49.3 to ~51.8**. The biggest implementation correction is therefore **not** “change strategy”; it is **pick the actual town and flat spec now**, because at today’s asking prices **€200k buys about 71.5 m² in Benicàssim, 90.7 in Peñíscola, 99.8 in Oropesa, and 118.8 in Vinaròs**.

Tourist-rental tightening is mildly favorable to your strategy, but I would not over-credit it. In the Valencian Community, tourist-rental registrations now require a municipal compatibility report and expire after five years unless renewed; nationally, since **3 April 2025**, new tourist lets need express community approval. Those changes probably **reduce the investor bid at the margin**, but I found no hard evidence yet that they are enough to pull premium-coast price growth back to your old baseline. ([BOE][4])

### 2) Investment return assumptions: 6% baseline

This is where I think your implementation plan is most biased. **MSCI World is currently expensive and U.S.-heavy**: as of **27 February 2026**, MSCI World showed **24.13 trailing P/E**, **19.91 forward P/E**, and about **70.1% U.S. weight**. Your chosen Fidelity fund is essentially the same exposure: around **70.1% U.S.**, accumulating, physically replicated, and explicitly notes that returns may rise or fall with currency moves. For a euro liability (future flat price) funded from euro income, that means you are taking a lot of **U.S. equity + USD FX** risk in the accumulation phase. ([MSCI][5])

The big-house outlooks are not screaming “6% nominal whole-portfolio base case” from here. Vanguard’s **VCMM**, published in January 2026, put **U.S. equities at roughly 3.9%–5.9% nominal annualized over 10 years**. BlackRock’s late-2025 capital-market outlook emphasized **elevated valuations**, **higher-for-longer rates**, and future returns tempered by the starting point. GMO’s **February 2026** 7-year forecasts were harsher still, with **negative real expected returns for U.S. large caps** and only modest real prospects for international large caps depending on the rate regime. None of that means 6% nominal is impossible over 15 years. It means **using 6% as the “middle box” in March 2026 is aggressive, not neutral**. ([Vanguard][6])

My correction: use **~5% nominal as the working baseline** for the **whole portfolio**, not 6%, and keep **4%** as a fair pessimistic case. On simple arithmetic, an **80/20** portfolio only gets to **6%** if the equity sleeve earns roughly **6.75%** while bonds earn **3%**; that is richer than the current valuation-based forecasts justify. Direction of bias: your original timeline is **too early**. Quantified impact: with **€1,500/mo**, **€7k initial**, and the same tax mechanics as your original model, moving from **6% / 2.5%** to **5% / 3%** pushes the **€200k** purchase from roughly **age 47.8 to 49.3** and the **€250k** purchase from roughly **50.7 to 53.1**. At **4.5% / 3%**, those become roughly **49.7** and **53.9**.

### 3) The €500/mo rent assumption

This is the **single most fragile live assumption** in the whole plan. Latest municipality-level asking rents are roughly **€10.5/m² in Benicàssim**, **€9.3 in Oropesa**, **€8.8 in Peñíscola** and **€8.4 in Vinaròs**, with YoY growth ranging from about **+5.9% to +13.1%** in the latest available prints. At those levels, **€500/mo** is still possible, but usually only at the **floor end** of the market: small, older, or less-premium stock. And the live listing pages across these coastal towns are full of **“septiembre-junio,” “octubre-mayo,” and “temporada”** wording, which is exactly the wrong kind of inventory for a 10–15 year execution plan. ([Idealista][7])

The legal backdrop cuts both ways. Your in-contract rent increases are limited by the current **INE rent-update reference index**, and I found **no Castellón municipalities** currently declared as “stressed areas,” which confirms that **between-contract resets are not capped** by the tension-zone regime. So the plan is protected against runaway increases **inside** a contract, but exposed to **market resets at renewal/change of flat**, exactly as you worried. ([BOE][8])

My verdict: **€500/mo is not impossible, but it is not robust enough to be the base execution assumption**. For planning, I would treat **€500 as upside**, **€650 as the real working case**, and **€800 as the stress case**. Direction of bias: the current plan probably **overstates sustainable DCA**. Quantified impact: if every extra euro of rent reduces DCA one-for-one, a permanent **€150/mo** rent hit delays the purchase by about **1.25 years** on the **€200k** path and **1.5 years** on the **€250k** path; a **€300/mo** hit delays them by about **2.75** and **3.33 years**.

### 4) Tax and regulatory verification

The core tax/regulatory assumptions still stand. As of **16 March 2026**, AEAT still shows the savings-base scale at **19% / 21% / 23% / 27% / 30%**. CNMV’s January 2026 funds guide still confirms that **traspasos** defer taxation until final redemption, that **FIFO** applies, and that the intermediary only withholds **19%** on gains at sale even though the final IRPF liability is progressive. Comunitat Valenciana’s ITP/TPO cut to **9% from 1 June 2026** is official, and the current Valencian wealth-tax minimum exempt remains **€1,000,000**, with the **€300,000** habitual-residence exemption still in place. ([www3.agenciatributaria.gob.es][1])

Two implementation nuances matter more than the headline rates. First, the ITP change is **real but small**: updating total purchase costs from **10% to 9.5%** only accelerates your baseline by about **one month**, not years. Second, the **cash-flow timing of tax** matters. Because the sale withholding is only **19%**, you can still owe a meaningful top-up at the next Renta campaign. In the same baseline mechanics that reproduce your file’s purchase ages, I estimate that top-up at roughly **€2.8k** for the €200k case and **€5.3k** for the €250k case. So you need a **post-sale tax reserve**, not just “enough on notary day.” ([cnmv.es][9])

There is also a real audit correction here: one of your files says the year-12 baseline involves about **“€267K gain”** and **“€60–65K tax.”** That is internally inconsistent. In your own table just above it, **€267K** is the **appreciated flat value**, not the portfolio gain. Re-running the same model that reproduces your purchase dates gives gains closer to roughly **€98k** on the €200k path and **€162k** on the €250k path, with taxes far below €60k in those cases. That looks like a **narrative bug, not a table bug**, but it matters because it can distort how you think about liquidation drag. 

The **IVF guarantee** is now a stronger contingency option than in your original mortgage analysis: the 2026 call is open, the age limit is **up to 45**, the guarantee now reaches **20%** of price, the property cap rose to **€311,000**, and the program budget was expanded in **February 2026**. But there is a catch: because you are **36**, this fallback disappears in about **nine years**. So the IVF improvement is useful only if you run a contingency review **well before** your projected cash-buy date. ([GVA][10])

### 5) Platform and fund risk: Trade Republic + Fidelity + Vanguard

The **fund choices themselves** are still defensible. Fidelity MSCI World Index Fund **IE00BYX5NX33** is still a low-cost, accumulating, Spain-registered core fund with **0.12% ongoing charges**. Vanguard Global Bond EUR Hedged Acc **IE00B18GC888** is still an appropriate broad **EUR-hedged** bond sleeve and carries **0.15% ongoing charges**. The bond-fund hedge is correct; for bonds, you do not want to add extra currency noise. ([fidelityinternational.com][11])

The bigger issue is **platform execution risk**, not solvency risk. Trade Republic’s Spain launch materials do confirm **Spanish IBANs**, **automated withholding** on gains and interest, **mutual funds with traspasos**, and no custody fees. Current public support still makes cash, ETF and securities workflows much more explicit than fund workflows, and I could **not independently confirm public-side** that both of your exact ISINs are currently enabled for your specific Spanish account because the catalogue is account- and app-rendered. I did, however, verify that TR publicly exposes a Spain mutual-fund browse path and that it states client securities remain **your property**, while cash benefits from deposit protection at partner banks up to the usual limit. My read: **the real TR risk is operational friction and support quality, not “broker blows up and your fund disappears.”** ([assets.traderepublic.com][12])

MyInvestor remains the cleaner backup—and maybe the cleaner primary platform if you optimize for **low-friction ADHD execution**. It publicly advertises **no custody, purchase/sale, transfer or change-of-commercialiser fees** on funds, and it has a much more detailed public FAQ around recurring orders, cut-offs and traspasos. It also appears to market BlackRock’s **iShares Developed World Index Class S** at **0.06%**, versus your Fidelity option at **0.12%**, although I could not fully verify the live retail order flow outside login. The important part is prioritization: a **6 bps** fee saving is only about **€1.2k over 12 years** or **€2.0k over 15 years** at €1,500/mo in my math. One **€150/mo rent reset** costs **€21,600** over 12 years before inflation. So do **not** let a TER race distract you from rent risk and platform friction. ([myinvestor.es][13])

One hard correction: the Vanguard bond fund is **not** a safe purchase bucket. Its official factsheet shows a **-15.1%** calendar-year loss in **2022**. So your current “10–20% bonds” idea is fine during long accumulation, but it is **not enough** once the flat purchase becomes plausible. Within roughly **24 months** of a likely purchase, money earmarked for notary day needs to be in **cash or a monetary/cash-equivalent vehicle**, not just a global aggregate bond fund. ([fund-docs.vanguard.com][14])

### 6) The buy-later timeline: how robust are age 48 / 51?

Your baseline table is reproducible. Using the same monthly mechanics as your file—**€7k lump sum, €1,500/mo DCA, progressive Spanish tax on gains, and a moving property target**—I can reproduce roughly **11.8 years** to the **€200k** path and **14.7 years** to the **€250k** path under the original **6% return / 2.5% property** baseline. 

The issue is not arithmetic; it is the central assumption set. Under a more realistic 2026 working case of **5% nominal return**, **3% property growth**, and **9.5% total purchase costs**, the purchase ages shift to about **49.3** for **€200k** and **53.1** for **€250k**. Under **4.5% / 3%**, they move to about **49.7** and **53.9**. If rent drift cuts DCA from **€1,500 to €1,200**, the same **5% / 3%** case moves to roughly **52.9** and **58.0**.

This is where “buy later” can become “functionally never.” In my rerun, **€250k** with **€1,200/mo**, **4% returns**, and **4% property growth** does **not** hit the target even within a 100-year simulation horizon; with **€1,500/mo**, it still slips to about **age 63.5**. For me, the real red line is not mathematical impossibility; it is **retirement math degradation**. If purchase slips from about **51** to **58**, the years left to rebuild retirement capital by **69** fall from **18** to **11**, cutting projected liquid retirement savings from about **€518k to €262k** at 5% nominal with the same €1,500/mo. That is the point where the plan stops being “delay ownership” and starts being “compress retirement.”

### 7) Risks the analysis underweights or misses

**Late-stage sequence risk is still underweighted.** With no glidepath, a roughly **25%–30% portfolio hit** near the purchase window can delay the **€200k** path by about **2.4–2.8 years** and the **€250k** path by about **2.9–3.5 years** in my reruns. Even a more defensive **~18%** hit still costs about **1.7–2.0 years**. The reason is simple: your bond sleeve is not a cash substitute, and the official Vanguard factsheet already showed that in 2022. ([fund-docs.vanguard.com][14])

**Climate and flood risk are not abstract 2050 risks on this coast; they are a live micro-location filter now.** The official screening tools are **PATRICOVA** and the national **SNCZI** flood maps. The October **2024 DANA** was extreme, AEMET reported extraordinary rainfall conditions, and later research tied the event to materially more intense precipitation under climate change. Castellón also received special heavy-rain warnings again in **2025**. That does **not** mean “don’t buy on the coast.” It means your province-wide price model is incomplete unless your eventual shortlist excludes bad flood micro-zones and bad building types. ([miteco.gob.es][15])

**Income risk matters more than your spreadsheet admits.** I do not think “remote engineer” is a doom scenario—WEF still ranks software and applications developers among the faster-growing occupations—but current institutional work is clear that AI is reshaping high-skill work: the IMF says nearly **40% of global jobs** are exposed to AI-driven change, the OECD finds employment growth has been much weaker in higher-automation-risk jobs than in low-risk ones, and the ILO’s 2025 update still sees a large share of jobs exposed to transformation rather than simple replacement. For your plan, that means one thing: **do not assume DCA grows smoothly with income**, and do not run the emergency buffer too lean while your housing costs are still externalized. ([IMF][16])

**The IVF age deadline is a hidden option expiry.** Because the improved guarantee only runs to **age 45**, your contingency set is stronger than before—but only for the next nine years. If you might ever pivot to a part-cash / part-mortgage fallback, that is a decision for your early 40s, not your late 40s. ([GVA][10])

**A capex/furnishing reserve is still missing from the model.** This is not fatal, but it is real. Adding a **€10k–€20k** reserve for furniture, remedial works or immediate upgrades delays the purchase by roughly **0.3–0.8 years** in my reruns. That is smaller than rent or return risk, but it is not zero.

## Monitoring triggers

* **Housing-cost trigger:** if your all-in rent for a real long-term lease rises above **€650/mo**, or if DCA drops below **€1,350** for more than **6 months**, rebase the timeline immediately.
* **Town-price trigger:** if the chosen town/flat spec starts behaving like **Benicàssim/Peñíscola** rather than **Oropesa/Vinaròs**, or if your chosen spec clearly migrates from the **€200k** bucket to the **€250k** bucket, update the target rather than pretending the original range still means the same thing.
* **Purchase-age trigger:** if the projected cash-buy age for your chosen spec rises above **55**, treat that as a plan review point. Beyond that, the post-purchase retirement accumulation window starts shrinking too fast.
* **De-risking trigger:** once **after-tax portfolio / required cash target** exceeds about **70%**, start reducing equity. At **85%+**, move any money likely needed inside **24 months** into cash/monetary, not aggregate bonds.
* **IVF trigger:** at **age 42**, run a formal contingency review. If you might ever want a mortgage fallback, you need to decide before the **age-45 IVF cutoff**. ([GVA][10])
* **Property-selection trigger:** any shortlisted building in **PATRICOVA/SNCZI medium/high flood exposure**, or with obvious DANA vulnerability, should be excluded or heavily discounted. ([miteco.gob.es][15])

The shortest version of the audit is this: **your tax structure is fine, your platform/fund stack is acceptable, but your baseline is too rosy.** The three things to fix before sending money are **rent realism, municipality specificity, and a late-stage glidepath**.
