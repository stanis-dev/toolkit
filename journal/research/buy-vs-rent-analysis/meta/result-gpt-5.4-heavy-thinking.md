4) Tax and regulatory verification: are the rules still current?

Current data. The key Spanish-fund mechanics are still intact. CNMV’s January 2026 fund-tax guide confirms: gains sit in the savings base; the current rates are 19% / 21% / 23% / 27% / 30% by bracket; FIFO still applies; and traspasos still defer taxation for Spanish funds and EU UCITS registered with CNMV, while ETFs do not qualify. In Comunitat Valenciana, ITP on resale homes becomes 9% from 1 June 2026, with 11% above €1 million. The IVF first-home guarantee program was re-convoked for 2026 in the DOGV in January 2026, and IVF’s own site says the funding envelope was increased to €30 million. The big correction is wealth tax: Generalitat tax pages show the general Valencian exempt minimum is €500,000, not €1,000,000; the €300,000 primary-residence exemption comes from the state wealth-tax law. Also, the reduced 6% ITP for under-35s on habitual homes up to €180,000 is current, but you are 36, so it does not help you.

Audit. Most of your tax logic still stands. The serious correction is the wealth-tax threshold. That error does not break the accumulation plan immediately, but it means your later-life tax map is materially wrong earlier than you thought. The other thing to keep explicit is date sensitivity: your 9% ITP assumption is correct only for purchases from 1 June 2026 onward.

Direction of bias. Current tax bias is mildly too favorable because of the wealth-tax assumption. For the house-purchase event itself, your cost assumption is roughly fine.

Quantified impact. The wealth-tax correction does not move the purchase date today; it does move the threshold at which post-purchase tax planning matters. The ITP rate change is worth €2,000 on a €200k purchase and €2,500 on a €250k purchase versus a flat 10% purchase-tax assumption, but your own notes had already mostly incorporated the June-2026 9% rate.

7) Risks the analysis still underweights

Climate and building-specific risk. You correctly mention flood maps, but I think you still underweight the fact that this is an asset-selection filter, not just an annual insurance variable. Generalitat and MITECO both maintain flood-risk mapping systems (PATRICOVA and SNCZI), and the Generalitat’s DANA information pages were still being updated after the 2024 floods. On the coast, this can feed into future insurance availability, community-capex surprises, and buyer demand for older buildings in exposed zones.

Tourist-rental regulation spillovers. Short-term rental regulation is getting denser, not lighter. The Valencian government has been cleansing the tourism registry, and the national short-term rental framework now includes a Registro Único and a Ventanilla Única Digital, with a 2025 order approving the reporting model. That does not prove a big positive spillback into long-term supply on the Castellón coast, but it does mean the tourist-rental side is not a stable constant anymore.

FX and U.S. concentration. You are buying “global equities,” but what you are really buying is a heavily U.S.-dominated, mega-cap-concentrated, unhedged developed-market equity stream. That is fine; it is just not the same thing as “broad world growth at 6% in euros.”

Behavioral simplicity. For someone with ADHD, your current implementation still has too many knobs: monthly DCA, cash bucket, equity sleeve, bond sleeve, rebalancing bands, platform backup, tax-state changes, rent monitoring, and purchase-timing flexibility. That is manageable only if the rules are brutally simple. The biggest unpriced risk is not panic-selling; it is decision fatigue that leads to inconsistent contributions or endless tinkering.
