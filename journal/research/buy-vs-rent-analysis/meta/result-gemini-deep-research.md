Deep Research: Pre-Execution Assumption Audit and Implementation Stress Test
Executive Assessment
The strategic decision to pursue an "invest now, buy later without a mortgage" pathway is mathematically sound in a theoretical vacuum, but the specific implementation plan designed for execution on the Castellón coast in early 2026 exhibits severe structural fragilities that jeopardize the entire endeavor. An exhaustive, adversarial audit of the underlying assumptions reveals that the execution parameters rely on a confluence of idealized conditions that fundamentally misrepresent the current macroeconomic, regulatory, and housing reality of the Valencian Community. The most critical failure point is the assumption of a €500 monthly rent for a primary residence, which empirical market data from the first quarter of 2026 demonstrates is practically non-existent for year-round contracts, thereby masking a severe forthcoming reduction in the monthly dollar-cost averaging capacity. Furthermore, the selection of Trade Republic as the primary custody platform introduces acute operational and fiscal risks due to documented failures in its Spanish investment fund transfer infrastructure, threatening the tax-deferral compounding mechanism that forms the absolute bedrock of this strategy. While the long-term thesis remains superior to heavily leveraged early-stage buying under current interest rates, committing capital to the existing implementation plan without immediate recalibration of the rental baseline, timeline expectations, and platform architecture presents an unacceptably high probability of systemic mathematical underperformance and behavioral fatigue.
Assumption Audit
1. Property Price Assumptions: Market Divergence and Climate Repricing

However, utilizing regional aggregate data masks extreme localized hyper-segmentation on the Castellón coast in early 2026, rendering the 2.5 percent baseline assumption dangerously conservative for the specific target municipalities of Benicàssim, Oropesa del Mar, and Peñíscola.
Current market data from February and March 2026 indicates that the Castellón coastal market is experiencing accelerated and highly asymmetric appreciation. According to recent transactional and asking price indexes from Idealista and Fotocasa, Peñíscola has registered a staggering 16.9 percent year-over-year increase, reaching an average of €2,204 per square meter by February 2026.1 Benicàssim continues to command a steep coastal premium, with average prices hitting €2,796 per square meter, and highly sought-after micro-locations such as Torreón–La Almadraba reaching €3,877 per square meter after a nearly 15.9 percent monthly surge.2 In contrast, Vinaròs presents a more stagnant profile, averaging €1,890 per square meter, reflecting a market highly dependent on domestic, non-premium buyers.3

While a 6.0 percent nominal return is historically defensible over rolling thirty-year periods, utilizing it as a baseline for a ten-to-fifteen-year horizon initiated in 2026 is mathematically precarious given current equity valuations.
The MSCI World Index is currently dominated by United States large-cap equities, which constitute roughly 70 percent of the index weight. Forward-looking capital market assumptions from major asset managers released in early 2026 indicate a structural deceleration in expected returns for this specific segment. Vanguard's 2026 Capital Markets Model explicitly projects annualized returns for United States equities to hover between 3.9 percent and 5.9 percent over the next decade, heavily constrained by elevated Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings ratios and stretched profit margins.7 While global ex-U.S. equities show slightly higher expected yields of 7.3 percent to 9.3 percent due to more attractive entry valuations, the massive U.S. weighting of the chosen Fidelity MSCI World fund naturally drags the aggregate expected return closer to the 5.0 percent threshold.7

3. The €500/Month Rent Assumption: The Fatal Flaw
The single most critical execution dependency in the chosen strategy is the capacity to sustain a €1,500 monthly investment contribution, which is mathematically predicated on maintaining housing costs at a strictly controlled €500 per month. An exhaustive adversarial audit of the Castellón coastal rental market in the first quarter of 2026 demonstrates that this assumption is catastrophically disconnected from empirical reality and represents the highest probability of execution failure.
Data aggregated from major Spanish real estate portals reveals that long-term residential leases designed for permanent habitation at €500 per month on the Castellón coast simply do not exist in viable, executable volumes.10 While the provincial average rental price technically stands at 9.5 Euros per square meter—meaning €500 theoretically secures a 52-square-meter space—coastal dynamics heavily distort this average.4 The vast majority of properties listed at or near the €450 to €550 mark in municipalities like Peñíscola or Oropesa are explicitly marketed as seasonal rentals, available only from September to June.10 Landlords intentionally utilize this mechanism to circumvent the rigid price controls and mandatory five-year contract extensions dictated by the Spanish Urban Leasing Law.
Securing a genuine, year-round long-term contract in a municipality like Benicàssim, or premium areas of Oropesa and Peñíscola, currently demands a minimum baseline of €750 to €900 per month for a standard two-bedroom apartment.12 To execute the strategy at a strict €500 rent, the investor would be forced to endure extreme behavioral and lifestyle friction. They would have to either accept seasonal contracts—forcing an eviction every June to make way for summer tourists and requiring a temporary sublet during the peak season—or relocate far inland, entirely defeating the purpose of coastal living during the accumulation phase. For an individual diagnosed with ADHD, who typically requires strict environmental stability and routine to maintain professional performance as a remote engineer, the psychological toll of continuous housing precarity is an unacceptable execution risk that will inevitably lead to strategy abandonment.
Direction of bias in the original assumption: The €500 rent assumption is fundamentally broken and severely biases the entire mathematical model in favor of the renting and investing strategy.
Quantified impact on the execution plan: Correcting the rent assumption to a realistic €800 per month reduces the available monthly investment capital from €1,500 to €1,200. Over a fifteen-year period at a 5.0 percent return, this reduction in investment capital results in a terminal portfolio value of approximately €320,000 instead of the projected €400,000.
4. Tax and Regulatory Verification: A Shifting Fiscal Landscape
The execution plan relies on tax and regulatory parameters modeled prior to recent legislative shifts. Several crucial parameters have undergone modifications in early 2026 that directly alter the fiscal math of the accumulation and eventual purchase phases, requiring immediate integration into the model.
First, the capital gains tax framework on savings income was expanded by Ley 7/2024. The current brackets are highly progressive and aggressively target significant wealth accumulation. The rates applicable in 2026 for Spanish tax residents are structured as follows: 19.0 percent on the first €6,000 of gain; 21.0 percent on gains from €6,000 to €50,000; 23.0 percent on gains from €50,000 to €200,000; 27.0 percent on gains from €200,000 to €300,000; and a punitive 30.0 percent for any gains exceeding the €300,000 threshold.15 While the tax-deferral mechanism of the investment fund transfer protects the portfolio during the fifteen-year accumulation phase, a massive liquidation event—such as selling off €250,000 worth of accumulated funds in a single tax year to purchase a property outright in cash—will trigger catastrophic fiscal drag. Under Spanish First-In, First-Out cost basis rules, the earliest shares purchased will carry the largest embedded gains, immediately pushing the investor into the 23.0 percent or 27.0 percent brackets and permanently destroying years of compounded returns in a single transaction.

5. Platform and Fund Risk: The Transfer Infrastructure Crisis
The strategy stipulates using Trade Republic as the primary broker to invest in the Fidelity MSCI World Index Fund, citing the platform's zero-fee automated savings plans and a 2.02 percent yield on uninvested cash.21 The Fidelity fund itself is an optimal vehicle; it boasts a highly competitive 0.12 percent Total Expense Ratio, accumulates dividends natively, and is physically replicated.24 Furthermore, the Vanguard Global Bond Index Fund remains a standard-bearing choice for the risk-dampening sleeve at a 0.15 percent Total Expense Ratio.26
However, the selection of Trade Republic as the primary custody platform introduces a critical point of failure that threatens to invalidate the core mathematical advantage of the strategy. The entire fiscal advantage of utilizing investment funds over Exchange Traded Funds in Spain hinges entirely on the transfer regime, which allows for tax-free rebalancing between qualifying funds. While Trade Republic launched mutual fund support in Spain in June 2025 specifically to capture this market, extensive peer and user reports through the first quarter of 2026 demonstrate severe, unresolved operational immaturity in their transfer infrastructure.28
Trade Republic utilizes an omnibus account structure based in Germany. Integrating this localized architecture with the complex Spanish SNCE and Allfunds transfer protocols has resulted in persistent, documented system errors. Users attempting to execute standard tax-free transfers routinely find their orders erroneously executed as taxable sell-and-buy transactions.29 For an investor relying on frictionless compounding over fifteen years, a platform that accidentally triggers a capital gains realization event due to backend application programming interface failures is a catastrophic execution risk. Attempting to reverse such an error with the Agencia Tributaria requires complex administrative appeals, generating immense bureaucratic friction entirely antithetical to an ADHD-friendly, set-and-forget mandate.
Furthermore, as of June 2025, Trade Republic officially deployed Spanish International Bank Account Numbers for all localized accounts, forcing automatic tax reporting directly to the Spanish tax authorities.30 This increases institutional visibility and makes accidental tax realizations via broken transfers impossible to mitigate or ignore during annual filings.
Direction of bias in the original assumption: The assumption that Trade Republic provides seamless, tax-free rebalancing and platform migration is dangerously flawed and operationally unverified.
Quantified impact on the execution plan: An erroneous transfer at year seven on a €150,000 portfolio could trigger an immediate, unplanned tax liability of €7,000 to €10,000 under the 21.0 percent bracket, permanently removing that capital from the compounding curve and delaying the ultimate purchase timeline by twelve to eighteen months.
6. The "Buy Later" Timeline: Volatility and Horizon Expansion
The projected timeline of buying a flat outright at age 48 for €200,000 or age 51 for €250,000 is highly fragile when subjected to the revised macroeconomic inputs established in this audit. The initial model mathematically solves for the intersection of a rapidly rising portfolio and a slowly rising property price. However, as established above, the execution capacity must be downgraded due to realistic rent constraints, the expected equity return must be moderated due to initial historically high valuations, and the property growth rate must be elevated due to regional supply constraints resulting from the PATRICOVA 3 directives.
When modifying the inputs to reflect a sustainable €1,200 monthly investment, a 5.0 percent nominal portfolio return, and a 4.0 percent localized property appreciation rate, the timelines drastically diverge. A premium coastal property priced at €250,000 today will cost approximately €450,000 in fifteen years. Simultaneously, a €1,200 monthly investment compounding at 5.0 percent will reach roughly €320,000 over the exact same period. The parallel lines of the strategy no longer intersect within the original ten-to-fifteen-year window.
This introduces a severe secondary risk. The timeline approaches the boundary of "never actually buying." If the required accumulation phase extends beyond twenty years, the investor will be approaching their late fifties. At this life stage, securing a mortgage as a fallback option becomes exceedingly difficult due to stringent age limits on loan maturation, as the vast majority of Spanish retail banks require mortgages to be fully cleared by age seventy-five. Consequently, the investor is forced into a corner where they must either liquidate the portfolio at a severe capital gains penalty to buy outright, drastically compromise on the quality of the asset, or accept permanent renter status—which carries massive inflation risk into retirement as rental yields continue to compound.
Direction of bias in the original assumption: The age 48 and age 51 estimates are highly optimistic best-case scenarios that fail to account for mandatory living expenses and macroeconomic drag.
Quantified impact on the execution plan: Under realistic market parameters, the outright purchase timeline shifts from late forties to late fifties, assuming the investor stubbornly refuses to utilize a partial mortgage to bridge the eventual accumulation gap.
7. Risks the Analysis Underweights or Misses Entirely
Beyond the primary mathematical inputs, the implementation plan fails to quantify several systemic and behavioral risks that could derail execution over a decade-long horizon.
First, the analysis completely ignores the climate repricing of the Castellón coast via the aforementioned PATRICOVA 3 legislation. The implementation plan treats coastal real estate as a homogenous, perpetually appreciating asset class. However, buying real estate outright concentrates massive wealth into a single physical, illiquid asset. If the eventual target property is situated in a zone that is subsequently redlined by insurance companies or local governments due to rising sea levels or future severe weather events, the terminal wealth of the investor could be obliterated overnight. Ironically, renting indefinitely actually provides superior optionality against localized climate risk, allowing the investor to migrate away from depreciating flood zones without taking a capital loss.
Second, the portfolio carries massive, unhedged currency exposure. The investor earns in Euros and intends to purchase a Euro-denominated physical asset. However, the Fidelity MSCI World Index Fund is heavily weighted in Dollar-denominated equities. The portfolio is therefore intrinsically short the Euro and long the Dollar. If the exchange rate experiences structural mean reversion or a prolonged period of Euro strength during the accumulation phase, the Euro-denominated value of the portfolio will suffer heavy drag, completely independent of the underlying equity performance.
Third, the strategy severely underestimates the regulatory friction in the rental market. Spanish urban leasing laws provide a five-year mandatory extension for natural-person landlords, with strict caps on inflation-linked annual increases. While this protects renters in the short term, it heavily incentivizes landlords to force evictions precisely at the five-year mark to reset the lease to higher market rates with a completely new tenant. Over a fifteen-year accumulation phase, the investor must mentally and financially prepare for a minimum of two to three forced relocations. The disruption, moving costs, and agency fees drain both capital and psychological energy, creating a high likelihood of behavioral fatigue for an ADHD investor seeking stability.
Fourth, the strategy fundamentally misprices the single-transaction liquidation penalty. The core tenet of "invest now, buy later without a mortgage" demands liquidating hundreds of thousands of euros in a single fiscal year. This triggers the maximum 27.0 percent and 30.0 percent capital gains brackets. A far more capital-efficient strategy at year fifteen is to leverage the portfolio by taking out a moderate, low loan-to-value mortgage to buy the house, paying the mortgage down through continued portfolio yield, and avoiding the massive tax realization event entirely.
