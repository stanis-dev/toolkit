# Owning a coastal flat in Castellón province and using the IVF guarantee: recurring costs and collaborating banks

## Scope, price band and key assumptions

This report focuses on **recurring (“ongoing”) ownership costs** for a **coastal flat in Castellón province (Comunitat Valenciana, Spain)** bought as a **habitual/permanent residence** for **€200,000–€250,000**, over a **30‑year horizon**. It **excludes mortgage payments and insurance** (as requested). It also covers the **Institut Valencià de Finances (IVF) “Programa de Garantías para la compra de vivienda”** and the banks currently listed as collaborating entities. citeturn58view3

Because Spanish municipal charges and building costs depend heavily on the **specific municipality, the property’s cadastral details, and the building/community budget**, many figures are best treated as **ranges**. Where I provide estimates, I flag the uncertainty and explain the assumptions.

## Municipal recurring taxes and charges

### IBI for coastal Castellón municipalities

#### What IBI is and how it is calculated

**IBI (Impuesto sobre Bienes Inmuebles)** is a municipal property tax paid (typically) **annually** to the town/city council.

For a residential flat, the foundational mechanics are:

* The **tax base** ultimately comes from the property’s **valor catastral** (cadastral value) set by the Catastro (Cadastre). For example, Vinaròs’ IBI ordinance states that the **base imponible** is the **valor catastral**, and the **cuota íntegra** results from applying the **tipo de gravamen** to the **base liquidable**. citeturn39view0turn37view0turn43view0  
* The **municipality sets the IBI rate** (tipo de gravamen) in its own ordinance. For instance, Castelló de la Plana’s ordinance lists a **general urban rate of 0.6492%**. citeturn37view0

In practice, your annual IBI bill is largely:

> **IBI ≈ (Base liquidable, usually derived from valor catastral) × (Municipal IBI rate)**  
(With any applicable bonifications/exemptions then reducing the final amount.)

#### What is a realistic valor catastral for a €200k–€250k flat?

It is important not to confuse **valor catastral** with market value:

* The cadastral valuation framework for urban property takes **market value as reference** and states it **cannot exceed market value**. citeturn54view0  
* A key national instrument for maintaining a consistent relationship to market value is the **coeficiente de relación al mercado (RM)**. A Spanish BOE order explicitly describes maintaining a relationship to market and states that the relationship is **expressly maintained at RM = 0.5**, and that the coefficient RM of **0.5** is applied to the individualised value resulting from the ponencia methodology (for qualifying ponencias). citeturn56view0  
  * Interpretation (practical): many “whole‑municipality” cadastral revisions are designed so that cadastral values are **about 50% of market** *at the time of that revision*, but the ratio can drift if market prices move faster than cadastral updates.

A useful reality check is how old some revisions can be. For example, the Oropesa listing shows **Año de Revisión Catastral: 2002**, which implies a long period for market/cadastral divergence to accumulate. citeturn49view0

**Working assumption for this report (explicitly uncertain):**

For a modern market value **€200,000–€250,000**, a plausible **current** valor catastral range for a coastal flat is often **~€60,000 to ~€150,000**, with a “central” planning range around **€100,000–€125,000** (consistent with a ~50% market relationship). This range is meant for budgeting only; the real figure depends on the property’s reference, size, location micro‑zone, building age, and the municipality’s last cadastral revision. citeturn56view0turn54view0turn49view0

#### Does IBI tend to increase over time and how often is valor catastral revised?

In a 30‑year horizon, it is prudent to treat IBI as **not fixed** because there are multiple moving parts:

* **Municipal rate changes**: councils can increase or reduce the rate. Benicàssim’s own communications show the rate moving (e.g., 0.60 → 0.58), illustrating that the “tipo” is a policy variable. citeturn45view2turn45view0  
* **Cadastral revisions / ponencias**: national valuation rules use market references and correction instruments (like RM) to maintain an intended relationship to market during revisions, and they also provide for modifications of ponencias. citeturn54view0turn56view0  
* **Update dynamics vary by municipality**: public discussion of cadastral update processes highlights that municipalities may request updates, and that requirements/conditions (e.g., minimum years since last revision) can matter in practice. citeturn21news45  

Bottom line: over decades, **IBI can rise or fall**, but most owners should assume **upward pressure** over time because both municipal budgets and valuation updates can change the bill (even if intermittently rather than annually). citeturn45view2turn56view0turn21news45

### Basura / waste collection and treatment charge

#### Is it a separate municipal charge in Castellón province?

Yes. In practice, Castellón municipalities frequently apply a separate charge/fee for municipal waste collection/treatment, and Spain’s **Ley 7/2022** created a strong driver for municipalities to adopt a **specific, differentiated, non‑deficit** waste charge that reflects the **real cost** of collection/transport/treatment (including indirect costs), within **three years** of the law’s entry into force. citeturn60view1

This is why many municipalities refer to a “new” waste fee/charge in recent years. citeturn60view1turn36view0turn36view1turn36view2

From municipality‑level sources:

## Building-level recurring costs

### Community fees (gastos de comunidad)

#### What they are and what drives the amount

Community fees are the regular amounts you pay to the **community of owners** (comunidad de propietarios) to fund ordinary shared expenses: cleaning, lighting, lift maintenance, pool and garden upkeep, building insurance (which you are excluding from your budgeting, but it is often paid at community level), administration, and routine repairs.

Two reliable “drivers” to treat as structural are:

* The fee is tied to the community’s **approved budget** and your **share/quota** (cuota de participación). citeturn34search3turn60view0  
* Amenities materially raise costs; for example, property‑market guidance highlights that a building **with a pool** will not have the same expenses as one **without a lift**, and vice versa. citeturn34search0

#### Typical monthly community fee expectations for a coastal flat in Castellón

Because we do not have a single official “tariff table” (each community sets its own budget), the best you can do pre‑purchase is treat this as a **scenario range** and verify against the community’s last annual accounts and minutes (see also the “derrama” section).

A practical budgeting range for Castellón coastal flats is:

* **Lower range (€40–€70/month)**: building with a lift and basic common elements, limited amenities, good maintenance history.  
* **Mid‑range (€80–€150/month)**: typical coastal complex with **lift + pool** (and often garden/common lighting), plus higher maintenance intensity. This is the “most common planning case” for a mid‑range coastal purchase with a pool. citeturn34search0turn34search3turn60view0  
* **Upper range (€150–€250+/month)**: larger developments and/or buildings with multiple lifts, extensive gardens, concierge/security, higher electricity consumption, ageing infrastructure, or significant service contracts.

Over a 30‑year horizon, owners should assume community fees **tend to rise** with general cost changes (labour, energy, service contracts) and the building’s ageing profile—sometimes smoothly, sometimes via step changes when contracts renew or maintenance needs grow. (For illustration of “step change” dynamics, municipalities themselves explicitly reference energy and contract cost pressures when discussing rate decisions, even though this is municipal rather than community budgeting.) citeturn45view0

### Derramas (special assessments) and long-term major works budgeting

#### What derramas are and how they work

A **derrama** is an **extraordinary charge** demanded from owners to fund **extraordinary expenses** not covered by ordinary community fees and/or the reserve fund—typically major repairs, rehabilitation, accessibility works, or large replacements.

Even with good governance, derramas occur because:

* Spanish horizontal property law places ongoing conservation/repair responsibilities on the community and requires owners to contribute to common expenses and a **reserve fund** to attend conservation/repair/rehabilitation and accessibility works. citeturn60view0  
* Major works can outstrip what the ordinary budget can accumulate quickly, especially if the building has not built up reserves or faces a time‑critical repair.

As a behavioural signal, companies active in façade rehabilitation explicitly note that derramas frequently cause community conflict and budgeting stress—reflecting how common extraordinary funding needs can be. citeturn58view1

#### Typical order-of-magnitude costs for common building works

There is no single “Castellón coastal price list”; costs depend on height, access to façades, extent of damage, tender competition, and whether energy-efficiency upgrades are bundled. Still, the sources below provide **useful Spanish market reference points** for budgeting.

**Façade rehabilitation (including common insulation systems)**  
A published benchmark range for façade rehabilitation is **€30–€120 per m²**, with examples by system type (e.g., monocapa at the low end; SATE / ventilated façades higher). citeturn58view1  

What this means per flat depends on the building’s façade area and number of units. Illustratively (simple arithmetic): if a building has ~600 m² of façade area, total range **€18k–€72k**; split across 20 flats that’s roughly **€900–€3,600 per flat** (often payable as a single derrama or staged instalments approved by the community).

**Roof / terrace waterproofing and leak repair**  
Habitissimo provides example budgets such as:  
* “Impermeabilizar cubierta” **€2,100 for 100 m²**  
* “Reparar goteras e impermeabilizar cubierta” **€2,500 for 100 m²** citeturn60view3  

These examples imply a rough **€21–€25 per m²** order of magnitude for common waterproofing scopes, which aligns with construction price‑generator references giving unit‑price figures in the same band (example unit reference: **€24.51** for a flat-roof waterproofing repair item). citeturn60view3turn57search3  

**Elevator installation / major lift works (replacement or new installation)**  
Open, accessible sources in this research session did not provide a clean, citable national €/unit benchmark for **elevator replacement** specifically. However, elevator works are routinely treated as **high‑ticket community interventions** (often bundled into broader rehabilitation projects). As an illustration of scale, one municipal rehabilitation project (not a private community derrama, but still informative on construction budgets) listed a total budget of about **€146,734** for a building intervention that included **roof replacement, façade rehabilitation and installing a lift**. citeturn57news44  

For a buyer, the practical takeaway is that **lift-related derramas can be material** and you should treat “lift condition + recent inspection/maintenance history + upcoming modernisation requirements” as a core due‑diligence item (reviewing last community minutes is usually more valuable than any generic benchmark).

#### A practical annual reserve to budget (30‑year horizon)

A sensible way to plan, consistent with the way Spanish communities must operate:

* Expect that part of your ordinary community fee goes to a legally-required **reserve fund** for conservation/repair/rehabilitation and certain accessibility works. citeturn60view0  
* Then add a personal “owner’s capital reserve” line for extraordinary works. A pragmatic planning approach (because coastal conditions can stress building envelopes and common elements) is to budget a **non‑zero annual average** for derramas even if the building looks well maintained on day one.

Given the available sources, the most defensible approach is to use your building’s **actual minutes, reserve fund balance, and planned works** to calibrate this line, and treat generic figures as placeholders.

In the IVF communication that lists the 14 entities, **no specific mortgage rates or product sheets** are published alongside the bank list; the communication is focused on programme eligibility and participating entities. citeturn58view3
