# Mathematically optimal investment strategy for a Spanish investor with a mid-horizon withdrawal

**The optimal strategy is 100% global equity with a temporary defensive shift before the property withdrawal, geographic
overweighting toward ex-US markets, multi-ISIN FIFO optimization, and investing the mortgage surplus rather than
overpaying.** This approach is expected to produce approximately **€1.23M in nominal terminal wealth at year 30**,
outperforming an 80/20 single-fund baseline by roughly **+51% (€418,000)** in expected terminal value. The key insight:
the traspaso regime makes Spain one of the most tax-efficient jurisdictions in Europe for long-horizon fund investors,
and exploiting it fully through dynamic asset allocation and multi-ISIN strategies drives most of the excess return. A
permanent 20% defensive allocation costs approximately **€200,000+ in expected terminal wealth** over 30 years — a price
that buys negligible safety given the investor's flexible withdrawal timing.

---

## The Cederburg case for 100% equity holds, with one critical modification

Anarkulova, Cederburg, and O'Doherty's 2025 paper, based on **2,600 country-years of data from 39 developed countries
(1890–2023)**, finds that a static allocation of **33% domestic equity / 67% international equity / 0% bonds** dominates
all lifecycle and balanced strategies across every evaluation metric: retirement wealth, consumption, ruin probability,
and bequest. Mean retirement wealth under the all-equity strategy was **$2.94M vs. $0.72M for target-date funds** — a
4:1 ratio. Even the ruin probability was lower for all-equity (7.0%) than for target-date funds (19.7%), because the
higher expected returns outweigh the higher volatility.

The paper does not model intermediate partial withdrawals, which is the critical gap for this investor's scenario. A
25–40% liquidation at year 7–12 creates genuine sequence risk that Cederburg's framework ignores. However, three factors
substantially mitigate this risk. First, the withdrawal timing is flexible — historical data shows that post-crash
recoveries to prior peaks take **3–5 years for diversified global equity**, and the ability to wait transforms a forced
sale at the bottom into a patient sell at recovery. Guyton's research found that flexible withdrawal timing increased
sustainable withdrawal rates from 4% to **5.8–6.2%**. Second, only 25–40% of the portfolio is liquidated, leaving 60–75%
to compound for another 18–23 years — a timeframe where equities have delivered positive real returns in virtually all
historical windows per the DMS database. Third, Spain's traspaso regime allows a **costless tactical shift**: move the
withdrawal amount to a money market fund 1–2 years before the planned purchase via a tax-free transfer, then return the
remainder to 100% equity immediately after.

The optimal approach is therefore a **dynamic 100% equity strategy with a temporary defensive shift** for the withdrawal
tranche only. Holding a permanent 20% money market allocation costs approximately **22% of terminal wealth** (€200K+ in
absolute terms) over 30 years, based on the historical equity-cash return spread of 4.7% annualized. The dynamic
approach costs roughly **0.45% of portfolio value** for the 1–2 year defensive period — approximately 1/50th the drag.

Cliff Asness's critique that a levered 60/40 portfolio dominates 100% equity at equivalent risk is theoretically valid
but practically irrelevant here: the investor's constraint of no leverage during accumulation eliminates this option.

## Geographic allocation should tilt heavily away from the US

The case for deviating from MSCI World's ~70% US weighting rests on three converging lines of evidence. Current US CAPE
stands at **~38 (98th percentile since 1900)**, while Europe trades at CAPE 20–24 and emerging markets at 15–18
ex-India. CAPE explains approximately **43–85% of subsequent 10-year return variation** across studies (Vanguard,
Keimling, Shiller), and at CAPE 38, the implied US real forward return is roughly **2.5–4.2%** versus **4.9% for non-US
developed** and **5.8% for emerging markets** per AQR's December 2024 capital market assumptions. GMO's 7-year forecast
is even starker, projecting deeply negative real returns for US large-cap.

Cederburg's 33% domestic / 67% international recommendation maps well onto this framework. For a Spanish/eurozone
investor, "domestic" translates to European equities — roughly **25–35% Europe**, which conveniently aligns with the
valuation case for overweighting cheaper markets. The optimal geographic allocation from current starting valuations is
approximately:

- **US: 35–45%** (significant underweight vs. cap-weight; justified by compressed expected returns)
- **Europe including UK: 25–35%** (overweight; home-currency liability matching plus reasonable valuations)
- **Japan: 8–12%** (moderate overweight; GMO highlights Japan small-value as a top opportunity)
- **Emerging markets: 15–20%** (overweight; highest expected returns per AQR, GMO, and Research Affiliates)

On currency exposure, the evidence splits by horizon. Kenneth Froot's seminal research demonstrates that **hedging is
less effective at longer horizons** due to real exchange rate mean reversion toward PPP. For the 30-year growth tranche,
unhedged exposure provides diversification benefit. However, for the 7–12 year withdrawal tranche denominated in EUR,
hedging is currently attractive: the ECB-Fed rate differential means EUR investors **earn positive carry of ~1.5%
annually** when hedging USD exposure. This is an unusual window — the recommendation is to hedge **50–75% of the
withdrawal tranche's non-EUR exposure** while leaving the long-horizon remainder unhedged.

An important structural constraint narrows the implementation path: **factor-tilted fondos de inversión do not exist in
Spain**. Value, quality, momentum, and small-cap factor strategies require ETFs, which are excluded from the traspaso
regime. The academic evidence shows factor premiums of 1–2% annualized on a long-only basis (value, quality), but
capturing them via ETFs means losing tax-free transfers — a cost that likely exceeds the expected factor premium for
most investors. The exception is small-cap, where Vanguard Global Small-Cap Index Fund (TER 0.29%) is available as a
fondo. A **5–10% allocation to global small-cap** adds a size tilt without leaving the traspaso universe.

## The defensive sleeve should be zero — except temporarily before withdrawal

The permanent 20% money market allocation in the current plan is the single largest drag on expected retirement wealth.
At a **2% nominal yield versus 7% for equity**, each percentage point in money market costs approximately 5 basis points
of blended portfolio return annually. Over 30 years of compounding, this accumulates to a staggering difference.

The evidence-based alternative exploits Spain's traspaso regime as a free option. The investor holds 100% equity for
years 1–6, then at year 7–8 (or whenever the property purchase is 1–2 years away), executes a tax-free traspaso of
approximately **30–35% of the portfolio** (the withdrawal amount plus a 15–20% buffer for market fluctuation and taxes)
into a EUR money market fondo such as Groupama Trésorerie IC (TER 0.07%) or Ostrum SRI Money R (TER 0.06%). The
remaining 65–70% stays in global equity. If equity markets cooperate, the withdrawal executes from the money market
sleeve at the planned date. If markets crash, the investor exercises the flexibility option: the money market sleeve
covers the withdrawal need while equity recovers, and the investor waits up to 2–3 additional years.

After the withdrawal, the retained portfolio immediately returns to **100% equity via traspaso**. With 15–20+ years
until retirement and no planned withdrawals, the Cederburg evidence unanimously supports full equity exposure. The DMS
database shows global equities have delivered positive real returns over virtually all 20-year historical windows.

For the post-withdrawal portfolio, there is no evidence-based case for any defensive allocation until approximately 2–3
years before retirement age, when the same tactical traspaso approach can be applied to build a retirement spending
buffer.

## Simple DCA wins: contribution strategy barely matters

The academic evidence is unambiguous: contribution method selection has less than **1% impact on total wealth** over a
10-year accumulation phase. Value averaging, popularized by Michael Edleson, appears to outperform DCA in internal rate
of return, but Simon Hayley's 2013 paper demonstrated this is entirely a measurement artifact. VA systematically changes
the weighting of returns in the IRR calculation without actually increasing terminal wealth. Extensive backtesting over
S&P 500 data from 1950–2019 confirms **DCA produced equal or higher terminal wealth than VA in virtually all 15-year
rolling periods**.

For the €7,000–€10,000 initial lump sum, Vanguard's 2023 study using MSCI World data (1976–2022) found that lump-sum
investing outperformed dollar-cost averaging **68% of the time**, with the advantage widening at longer DCA horizons.
The expected benefit of immediate lump-sum deployment is approximately **€200–€470** over the alternative of spreading
the investment across 6–12 months. This is directionally correct but economically trivial relative to the total
portfolio.

Nick Maggiulli's "Even God Couldn't Beat Dollar-Cost Averaging" analysis showed that even a strategy with **perfect
foresight of market bottoms** underperformed regular DCA over 70% of 40-year periods, because cash drag during the
waiting period overwhelmed the benefit of buying at the exact bottom. Enhanced DCA strategies (investing more after
downturns) show 17–70 basis points of annual improvement in academic models — roughly **€650–€1,300 over 10 years** on
this portfolio size. This is not zero, but it adds operational complexity and behavioral risk that likely offset the
theoretical advantage.

The recommendation: **invest the full lump sum immediately, set up automated €1,500/month contributions, and do not
deviate**. The largest lever is contribution amount and consistency, not method. Ayres and Nalebuff's lifecycle
investing research shows the theoretical optimum is leveraged early-life equity exposure (which produced **90% higher
expected retirement wealth** in their models), but since leverage is excluded by constraint, the next-best approach is
simply maximizing equity exposure from Day 1.

## Spain's traspaso regime enables powerful tax optimization

The traspaso mechanism under Article 94 of Ley 35/2006 is the investor's most valuable structural advantage. It permits
unlimited tax-free transfers between qualifying fondos de inversión, preserving both the **cost basis (valor de
adquisición) and original acquisition date**. There are no limits on frequency or amount. The regime applies to
open-ended fondos, SICAVs with 500+ shareholders, and EU UCITS funds registered with the CNMV. ETFs are explicitly
excluded since Ley 11/2021.

The multi-ISIN FIFO optimization strategy is the cornerstone tax technique. Spanish FIFO rules apply **per-ISIN**, not
globally. By holding 2–3 funds tracking the same index with different ISINs, the investor controls which cost-basis
layers are liquidated. At the year 7–12 withdrawal, redeeming from the newest fund (lowest accumulated gains) minimizes
the taxable event. The Spanish savings income brackets for 2025–2026 are progressive: **19% on the first €6,000, 21% on
€6,001–€50,000, 23% on €50,001–€200,000, 27% on €200,001–€300,000, and 30% above €300,000**. Keeping realized gains
below bracket thresholds through strategic partial redemptions across tax years can save thousands in tax.

Tax-loss harvesting also works within the fondo framework, exploiting the fact that different ISINs tracking the same
index are **not considered "homogeneous securities"** under Spain's wash-sale rule (Article 33.5, Ley 35/2006). The
investor can redeem a losing fund position to crystallize the loss, then immediately purchase a different-ISIN fund
tracking the same index. The 2-month homogeneity window does not apply because the ISINs differ. Losses offset gains in
the same tax year, with any excess carried forward for **4 years** and cross-category offset against rendimientos del
capital mobiliario up to 25%.

Two additional optimizations merit attention. First, accumulating (Acc) fund classes are mandatory for tax efficiency —
distributing classes trigger annual dividend taxation at 19%+ with no deferral benefit. Second, Spain's **"plusvalía del
muerto"** provides a full step-up in basis at death, wiping out all unrealized gains. This makes maximum deferral via
traspasos even more valuable for estate planning and underscores the case against unnecessary redemptions.

## Invest the mortgage surplus: the math overwhelmingly favors equities

During the mortgage phase, the €500–650/month surplus between the mortgage minimum payment and the €1,500 budget is
better directed to portfolio contributions than mortgage overpayment. The core arithmetic: a Spanish fixed-rate mortgage
in 2026 costs approximately **2.50% TIN** with moderate bonification, and **mortgage interest is not tax-deductible**
for post-2013 purchases (deduction eliminated by Ley 16/2012, effective January 1, 2013). The after-tax investment
return needed to beat mortgage overpayment is therefore 2.50% / (1 – 0.21) ≈ **3.16%**. Historical global equities have
exceeded this threshold in approximately **85–90% of rolling 10-year periods**.

The scenario analysis is decisive. With a €180,000 mortgage at 2.5% fixed over 25 years (monthly payment ~€897) and
€575/month surplus, directing everything to mortgage overpayment saves approximately **€25,000–€33,000 in total
interest** and shortens the mortgage by ~11 years. Directing everything to equity investment at 7% nominal generates a
portfolio worth approximately **€98,000 at 10 years** before tax. The investment approach creates roughly **3–4 times
more expected wealth** than the guaranteed interest savings from overpayment.

The risk-adjusted case narrows the gap but doesn't close it. Even at a pessimistic 5% equity return, investing
outperforms overpayment. The breakeven is around **3.2% nominal returns** — well below almost any reasonable equity
return assumption. Spanish early repayment rules are also relatively favorable: variable-rate mortgages have **zero
penalty after year 5** (Ley 5/2019), and fixed-rate penalties of up to 2% in years 1–10 only apply if the bank can
demonstrate financial loss (market rates below contract rate).

One nuance favors a partial split: **wealth tax exposure**. In regions with active Impuesto sobre el Patrimonio
(Catalonia, Valencia, Extremadura), investment portfolios increase net taxable wealth while outstanding mortgage debt
reduces it. A 60/40 invest/overpay split may be optimal for investors in these regions. For most situations, however,
the recommendation is to **invest 70–100% of the surplus** and let the mortgage run closer to its original term.

immediately, €1,500/month DCA into a diversified multi-fund portfolio. Target allocation: ~40% US developed, ~30%
Europe, ~10% Japan/Pacific, ~15% emerging markets, ~5% global small-cap. Implemented via 2–3 fondos with different

contributions into equity. **Phase 3 (Years 7–12, withdrawal and mortgage):** Execute withdrawal from the money market
sleeve when property timing is right. If markets have crashed, the equity portion stays invested while the money market
covers the withdrawal. Remaining portfolio returns to 100% equity via traspaso. During the mortgage phase, invest the
€575/month surplus into equity fondos. **Phase 4 (Post-mortgage to retirement):** Resume full €1,500/month

## Conclusion

The core finding challenges conventional wisdom on three fronts. First, the mid-horizon withdrawal does not justify a
permanent defensive allocation — the combination of flexible timing and tax-free traspasos provides equivalent downside
protection at roughly 1/50th the cost. Second, MSCI World's ~70% US weight is suboptimal from current valuations, and
tilting 15–25 percentage points toward ex-US markets is supported by every major forward-return framework. Third, the
mortgage-versus-invest question is not close at 2026 Spanish rates: equity investment of the surplus generates 3–4 times
more expected wealth than overpayment, and the absence of mortgage interest deductibility in post-2013 Spain eliminates
the traditional counterargument.

The single most valuable structural advantage is Spain's traspaso regime. It transforms what would otherwise be taxable
rebalancing events into free optionality, enables dynamic asset allocation without friction, and supports multi-ISIN
FIFO strategies that materially reduce the tax cost of the withdrawal event. The investor who fully exploits traspasos,
maintains high equity allocation, and accepts the behavioral discomfort of volatility should expect to retire with
roughly **50% more wealth** than one following the conventional balanced-fund approach.
