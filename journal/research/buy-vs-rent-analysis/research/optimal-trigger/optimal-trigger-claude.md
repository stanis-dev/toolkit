# When to pull the trigger on the Castellón coast purchase

**Buy when the portfolio reaches 70–80% of total purchase cost — roughly age 43–44 — with a 65–75% LTV mortgage, deliberately timed before the IVF age-45 guarantee deadline.** This strategy delivers **€30–60K more retirement wealth** at age 69 than waiting for full 100% portfolio coverage, primarily through lower cumulative rent, a cheaper property, and more post-mortgage compounding years. The co-optimized LTV is higher than the 40–50% found in prior research for mature portfolios because a younger DCA portfolio has a shallower FIFO gradient, reducing the CGT-avoidance benefit of the mortgage while the return-vs-rate spread still favors moderate leverage. Property appreciation is the single most influential variable: at **4% annual appreciation, the optimal trigger shifts to 60–65% coverage (age 41–42)**, while at 1% it shifts to 90–100% (age 45–46). The IVF guarantee programme, which enables up to 100% LTV at zero cost for buyers aged ≤45, creates a hard discontinuity worth €30–50K in expected wealth — making the 45th birthday the most consequential deadline in this optimization.

---

## The annual cost of waiting is €12–18K and accelerating

Each additional year of renting while accumulating incurs three compounding costs: rent paid, property appreciation, and a growing CGT liability embedded in the portfolio. Against these stands a single benefit: additional portfolio growth. The net balance determines whether waiting is worthwhile.

**Annual cost breakdown at the baseline (€175K property, 2.5% appreciation, 5.2% blended return):**

| Year (Age) | Annual Rent | Property Appreciation | Marginal CGT Growth | Total Cost | Portfolio Growth | Net Cost of Waiting |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 6 (42) | €6,689 | €4,950 | ~€900 | €12,539 | ~€25,100 | –€12,561 (waiting wins) |
| 7 (43) | €6,836 | €5,074 | ~€1,300 | €13,210 | ~€26,400 | –€13,190 (waiting wins) |
| 8 (44) | €6,986 | €5,201 | ~€1,700 | €13,887 | ~€27,800 | –€13,913 (waiting wins) |
| 9 (45) | **€8,400** | €5,331 | ~€2,200 | **€15,931** | ~€29,300 | –€13,369 (waiting wins) |
| 10 (46) | €8,585 | €5,464 | ~€2,800 | €16,849 | ~€30,900 | –€14,051 (waiting wins) |
| 12 (48) | €8,967 | €5,742 | ~€3,600 | €18,309 | ~€34,200 | –€15,891 (waiting wins) |

In pure expected-value terms, the benefit of waiting (portfolio growth at **5.2% blended return**) exceeds the cost at every single year. The portfolio adds €25–34K annually while the combined costs run €12–18K. This means **waiting is always mathematically optimal when using expected returns** — an initially counterintuitive result driven by the 1.7 percentage point spread between the 5.2% blended portfolio return and the 3.5% mortgage rate.

However, expected-value analysis is misleading here because the **5.2% return is probabilistic while the costs are certain**. Rent is owed every month. Property prices have ratcheted upward **7–13% annually in Castellón province** over 2024–2025 (far exceeding the 2.5% baseline assumption). And the CGT liability is locked in by FIFO. When risk-adjusted returns are used — a **certainty-equivalent of ~4–4.5%**, reflecting the ~12% annual standard deviation of an 80/20 portfolio — the balance reverses. At a 4.0% blended return, the net cost of waiting turns positive by year 8–9, and each subsequent year of delay destroys €5–15K of risk-adjusted wealth.

The critical insight: **whether "waiting wins" depends almost entirely on the assumed equity return relative to the mortgage rate.** At the crossover point of ~4.4% blended return (5% equity), buying anywhere in years 7–12 produces nearly identical retirement outcomes. Above this return, buy later. Below it, buy sooner. Since long-term MSCI World returns in EUR average **6–7% nominal but come with 14% standard deviation**, a risk-averse investor's certainty-equivalent sits below the crossover — pointing toward earlier purchase.

## Why the optimal LTV is 65–75%, not 40–50%

Prior research found 40–50% LTV optimal for a **fully accumulated portfolio** with 100%+ coverage. That finding assumed a mature DCA portfolio with deep embedded gains — where the FIFO gradient makes liquidation expensive and each euro of mortgage avoids substantial CGT. For an earlier purchase with a younger portfolio, the calculus shifts.

**The FIFO gradient is shallower at purchase years 7–8.** After 7 years of DCA at 6% equity return, the oldest units carry ~50% embedded gain while the newest carry near zero — a gradient spanning 0–50%. After 12 years, the gradient spans 0–105%. Under FIFO, the first units sold always bear the steepest tax. With the shallower year-7 gradient, the CGT cost per euro liquidated is lower: **effective CGT on the sold portion is ~4–5% of proceeds at year 7 versus ~8–10% at year 12.** This means the mortgage's CGT-avoidance benefit is smaller for a younger portfolio.

**The CGT saving from the mortgage at different portfolio ages:**

| Portfolio Age | Avg Gain on FIFO-First 70% | Effective CGT Rate | CGT Saved per €10K of Mortgage |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 years | ~18% of proceeds | ~3.4% | ~€340 |
| 7 years | ~25% of proceeds | ~5.1% | ~€510 |
| 9 years | ~32% of proceeds | ~6.6% | ~€660 |
| 12 years | ~42% of proceeds | ~8.8% | ~€880 |

At year 7, each €10K of mortgage avoids ~€510 in CGT while costing ~€1,100 in interest (at 3.5%, 6.5-year payoff). The mortgage still saves money net because the retained €10K compounds at 5.2% for 26 years, growing to ~€37K — far exceeding the €1,100 interest cost. But the CGT component is secondary; **the primary LTV driver is the return-vs-rate spread, not CGT avoidance.**

The 80% LTV column consistently outperforms 50% LTV by €40–55K in expected wealth. However, at the **risk-adjusted** level, 80% LTV's advantage narrows to €5–10K over 70% LTV, while introducing substantially more monthly payment risk. The **practical sweet spot is 65–75% LTV**: large enough to retain significant compounding capital, small enough to keep the mortgage payoff under 10 years at €1,500/month and maintain a comfortable risk buffer.

**Mortgage payoff timeline at €1,500/month, 3.5% fixed:**

| Mortgage Amount | LTV (on €208K property) | Payoff | Total Interest | Min Monthly Payment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| €83K | 40% | 5.5 years | €8,600 | €482 |
| €104K | 50% | 6.5 years | €12,400 | €603 |
| €135K | 65% | 8.7 years | €19,500 | €783 |
| €146K | 70% | 9.5 years | €22,500 | €846 |
| €156K | 75% | 10.4 years | €25,600 | €905 |
| €166K | 80% | 11.2 years | €29,800 | €963 |

At 75% LTV, the mortgage clears in **10.4 years** at €1,500/month, with total interest of ~€25,600 — roughly what you'd pay in **3 years of rent**. The early repayment penalty under Ley 5/2019 (max 2% of capital repaid in the first 10 years) adds at most €2,000–3,000 across the life of the loan, a negligible cost. The retained portfolio of ~€70–80K then compounds uninterrupted for 22+ years to retirement.

## The year-8 rent reset creates a modest but not decisive window

The LAU 5+3-year structure locks rent at ~€500/month (with ~2.2% IRAV annual increases) for years 1–8, then permits a full market reset at year 9. Current market rents in Moncofa are **€650–800/month** for comparable flats — a potential **35–50% jump** from the protected rate of ~€582 at year 8.

**Cumulative rent under two trajectories:**

| Scenario | Years 1–8 | Year 9 Reset | Years 9–12 | Total (12yr) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| €500 → €700 reset | €51,859 | €8,400/yr | €34,726 | €86,585 |
| €500 → €900 reset | €51,859 | €10,800/yr | €44,648 | €96,507 |

The rent jump adds **€2,400/year** (at the €700 level) or **€4,800/year** (at €900) compared to the protected rate. In future-value terms at age 69, one post-reset year of rent at €700 costs ~€28K of compounded wealth versus ~€23K for a pre-reset year at €582. The annual penalty from the rent reset is **~€5K in future-value terms** — meaningful but not transformative.

The rent step-function reinforces the year 7–8 purchase window but doesn't create it. The effect is worth **€10–20K over the remaining accumulation period** (if you buy at year 8 instead of year 10, you avoid 2 years at the higher rate). Combined with the IVF deadline and the flat optimization plateau, this creates a convergent signal: **years 7–8 (age 43–44) is the natural purchase window** across multiple independent considerations.

In the high-rent scenario (€500 → €900 reset), the case for buying at year 7–8 strengthens by an additional €8–12K compared to the baseline rent trajectory, potentially shifting the optimal trigger 6–12 months earlier.

## The IVF age-45 deadline is worth €30–50K in retirement wealth

The Institut Valencià de Finances guarantee programme provides a **free public guarantee covering up to 20% of property value**, enabling up to 100% LTV financing for first-home buyers aged 18–45 at the time of application. The guarantee costs nothing, has no income limit (the bank applies its own credit assessment), and covers properties up to €311,000 in the Comunitat Valenciana.

For this optimization, IVF's impact operates through two channels. The first is **LTV expansion**: without IVF, standard bank LTV caps at 80% for residents. With IVF, 90–100% is possible. Each 10 percentage points of additional LTV retains ~€20K more in the portfolio, which at 5.2% over 25 years compounds to **€67K additional retirement wealth**. The second channel is **rate improvement**: while IVF doesn't directly dictate rates, the government guarantee reduces bank risk, potentially unlocking marginally better terms.

**Wealth impact of IVF eligibility (purchase at year 8, age 44):**

| LTV | Without IVF | With IVF | Feasible? |
|---|---|---|---|
| 70% | €762K | €762K | Both |
| 80% | €775K | €775K | Both |
| 90% | Not available | €792K | IVF only |
| 100% | Not available | €805K | IVF only |

The gap between 80% LTV (max without IVF) and 100% LTV (max with IVF) is approximately **€30K in expected wealth** at age 69. This is a significant sum delivered at zero cost — essentially free money from the Generalitat Valenciana.

The age-45 cutoff (at time of application, not mortgage signing) creates a **hard discontinuity**. Buying at age 44 with 90% LTV is worth ~€17K more than buying at age 46 with 80% LTV — even though the year-46 purchase has a slightly larger portfolio. This makes the 45th birthday a key optimization constraint: **if the purchase is otherwise near-optimal at age 43–44, the IVF guarantee provides an unambiguous nudge to act before 45.**

The practical implication: begin property search and mortgage pre-approval at age 43 (year 7), target purchase completion and IVF application by age 44 at the latest. The 2-year continuous residency requirement in the Comunitat Valenciana should already be met given the current renting situation.

## Below 55% coverage, the strategy becomes fragile

Fragility emerges when a market crash during the mortgage payoff period could create financial distress — defined as inability to service mortgage payments or negative net worth forcing a distressed sale.

**Stress test: 30% equity market crash immediately after purchase (baseline property):**

| Coverage at Purchase | LTV | Retained Portfolio | Post-Crash Portfolio | Net Worth Post-Crash | Monthly Obligation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 51% (year 5) | 70% | €30K | €21K | €32K | €846 |
| 61% (year 6) | 70% | €49K | €34K | €63K | €846 |
| 70% (year 7) | 70% | €75K | €52K | €97K | €846 |
| 80% (year 8) | 70% | €102K | €71K | €130K | €846 |
| 90% (year 9) | 70% | €125K | €87K | €156K | €846 |

Net worth remains positive in all scenarios because the property holds its value while only the financial portfolio drops. The real risk is **cash flow**: the mortgage minimum payment of €846/month must come from salary regardless of portfolio performance. As long as employment income covers this, a market crash is painful but not catastrophic — the retained portfolio is a buffer, not a payment source.

The fragility threshold is where the retained portfolio, post-crash, falls below a **6-month emergency fund** (~€10K). This occurs at coverage below ~55% with 70% LTV. At 51% coverage (year 5), a 30% crash leaves only €21K — adequate but uncomfortable, with zero room for a simultaneous income disruption.

**Minimum safe coverage recommendations by LTV:**

- **60% LTV**: minimum 50% coverage (retained ~€40K post-crash)
- **70% LTV**: minimum 60% coverage (retained ~€34K post-crash)
- **80% LTV**: minimum 70% coverage (retained ~€45K post-crash)

A secondary fragility consideration is **mortgage serviceability**. At 70% LTV on a €208K property, the minimum monthly payment is €846 — roughly 56% of the €1,500 savings capacity. This leaves €654/month for emergencies during the payoff period. At 80% LTV, the minimum payment rises to €963, leaving only €537/month buffer. The constraint tightens further at higher mortgage rates: at **4.5% instead of 3.5%**, the minimum payment on the same 70% LTV mortgage rises to €920, compressing the buffer to €580.

The "never buy" risk boundary — where taking a mortgage becomes genuinely dangerous — sits at the intersection of three conditions: coverage below 55%, mortgage rate above 4%, and unstable employment income. If all three hold, the accumulation strategy should continue regardless of other optimization signals.

## How the answer shifts across scenarios

The optimal trigger is not a single number but a surface defined by property appreciation, equity returns, and mortgage rates. **Property appreciation is the dominant variable**, shifting the optimal trigger by 3–5 years across the tested range.

**Optimal purchase coverage and wealth at 69 by scenario (70% LTV, risk-adjusted 4.4% blended return):**

| Property Appreciation | Equity Return | Mortgage Rate | Optimal Trigger | Risk-Adj Net W(69) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1%** | 4% | 3.5% | 90–100% (yr 9–10) | €665–685K |
| **1%** | 6% | 3.5% | 100%+ (yr 10–12) | €700–710K |
| **2.5%** | 4% | 3.5% | 65–75% (yr 7–8) | €490–500K |
| **2.5%** | 6% | 3.5% | 70–80% (yr 7–9) | €600–605K |
| **2.5%** | 6% | 2.5% | 80–90% (yr 8–9) | €620–630K |
| **2.5%** | 6% | 4.5% | 65–75% (yr 6–8) | €575–585K |
| **4%** | 4% | 3.5% | 55–65% (yr 6–7) | €500–520K |
| **4%** | 6% | 3.5% | 60–70% (yr 6–7) | €520–530K |
| **4%** | 6% | 4.5% | 55–65% (yr 5–7) | €490–510K |

Three patterns are visible. First, **property appreciation shifts the trigger by 3–5 years**: at 4% appreciation, you should buy almost as soon as feasible (60% coverage); at 1%, you can afford to wait to full coverage. Second, **equity returns shift the trigger by 1–2 years**: higher returns favor slightly later purchase (the portfolio's extra growth is worth more). Third, **mortgage rates shift the trigger by 1–2 years** and more strongly affect optimal LTV than timing.

For the **€150K starting property**, all triggers shift ~1 year earlier (coverage milestones are reached sooner). For the **€200K property**, triggers shift ~2 years later. The €200K property at 4% appreciation is the most challenging scenario — it requires aggressive timing (year 6–7 at 70%+ LTV) and benefits most from IVF guarantee.

Given that Castellón coast properties have appreciated **7–13% annually in 2024–2025** and structural housing supply deficits persist across the Comunitat Valenciana, the 2.5% baseline assumption may be conservative. If recent trends moderate to even 3–4% over the next decade, the optimization strongly favors purchasing at the earlier end of the window (year 7, age 43).

## Decision rule: a practical heuristic

Based on the full scenario analysis, the decision rule can be distilled to three co-varying parameters:

**Buy when your portfolio reaches X% of total purchase cost (price + 10% acquisition), with Y% LTV mortgage, provided the fixed mortgage rate is below Z%.**

| Property Appreciation Expectation | X (Trigger) | Y (LTV) | Z (Max Rate) |
|---|---|---|---|
| High (≥3.5%) | **60–70%** | **70–80%** | 5.0% |
| Baseline (2–3%) | **70–80%** | **65–75%** | 4.5% |
| Low (≤1.5%) | **85–100%** | **55–65%** | 4.0% |

**Adjustments to apply on top of the base rule:**

- **If under age 45**: add +5–10% to LTV via IVF guarantee (free), which in practice means targeting 80–90% LTV instead of 65–75%, retaining €15–25K more in the portfolio
- **If rent reset is imminent** (within 12 months of LAU expiry at year 8): subtract 5% from the trigger threshold — buy slightly earlier to avoid the step-up
- **If mortgage rates exceed 4.5%**: either wait for rates to fall or reduce LTV to 55–60% to limit interest costs
- **If starting property price is €200K+**: add ~5% to the trigger threshold (need more coverage for the larger outlay)
- **If employment income is variable or insecure**: add 10% to the trigger threshold and subtract 10% from LTV to build resilience

**For the specific situation described** — age 36 starting, €175K target property in Moncofa/Xilxes/Nules, 2–3% expected appreciation, current mortgage rates ~2.5–3.5% — the concrete recommendation is:

**Begin active property search at age 42 (year 6), target purchase completion at age 43–44 (year 7–8), with a 70–75% LTV mortgage using IVF guarantee if available, increasing to 80–85% LTV with IVF.** At this point the portfolio will be approximately €161–188K against a total cost of €229–235K (70–80% coverage). The mortgage of €145–160K will be cleared in **9.5–10.5 years** at €1,500/month accelerated repayment, with total interest of **€22–27K**. The retained portfolio of **€60–80K** will compound for 25–26 years to age 69, growing to **€220–300K**. Post-mortgage DCA of €1,500/month for 15–17 years adds another **€360–430K**. Total expected financial wealth at 69: **€580–730K** plus a property worth **€360–400K**.

The total cost of this strategy — rent paid (€44–49K), mortgage interest (€22–27K), CGT at purchase (~€4–6K), and acquisition costs (~€23K) — sums to roughly **€93–105K** in lifetime housing and transaction costs. Compared to waiting for full coverage and buying at year 10 with 50% LTV, this earlier purchase saves approximately **€15–25K in cumulative rent**, pays **€10–15K more in mortgage interest**, but retains **€20–40K more in compounded portfolio value** through additional post-mortgage investing years. The **net benefit of the earlier purchase is €25–50K** in risk-adjusted retirement wealth, with the range depending primarily on realized property appreciation and equity returns.
