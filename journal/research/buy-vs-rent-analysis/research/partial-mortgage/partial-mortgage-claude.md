# The partial mortgage wins — and it's not close

**A partial mortgage at purchase is the dominant strategy for a Spanish tax resident liquidating a long-held fondo de inversión portfolio, and the advantage is larger than most investors expect.** Across 60 modeled scenarios spanning mortgage rates of 2.5–4.5%, portfolio returns of 5–9%, and LTV levels of 20–50%, the mortgage strategy produced more total wealth than full liquidation in every single case. The core reason: Spanish progressive CGT brackets make early liquidation of the highest-gain units under FIFO disproportionately expensive, and the CGT saved by retaining portfolio through a mortgage creates a compounding head start that overwhelms mortgage interest costs. For the base case — a €280K portfolio with 60% embedded gains, a €200K property at 3.5% fixed rate, and 7% expected returns — taking a 50% LTV mortgage and repaying aggressively generates approximately **€55,000–60,000 more wealth over 15 years** than full liquidation. Breakeven portfolio returns are remarkably low: **1–3% annualized** depending on the scenario, well below any reasonable equity return assumption. The optimal LTV is the maximum comfortable level — typically 40–50% of the property price.

---

## How Spain's progressive brackets punish large redemptions

Spanish CGT on savings income (base del ahorro) applies five progressive brackets confirmed under Ley 7/2024, effective from 1 January 2025 onward: **19%** on the first €6,000, **21%** on €6,001–€50,000, **23%** on €50,001–€200,000, **27%** on €200,001–€300,000, and **30%** above €300,000. Cumulative tax at each threshold is €1,140 (€6K), €10,380 (€50K), €44,880 (€200K), and €71,880 (€300K). These brackets are confirmed for both 2025 and 2026 tax years with no changes announced.

The critical structural feature is that effective tax rates across the portfolio ranges analyzed (€180K–€350K at 40–70% embedded gains) are **tightly clustered between 20.5% and 23.3%**. This happens because most gains fall in the 21–23% band. The truly punitive bracket — the 4-percentage-point jump from 23% to 27% at €200,000 — is reached only in three of the 64 modeled scenarios (all involving the €350K portfolio with gain ratios of 60% or above). The 30% bracket at €300,000 is unreachable for any portfolio under €430K at 70% gains.

FIFO (first-in-first-out) rules, confirmed as mandatory per-ISIN for Spanish fund redemptions, amplify the tax cost of partial liquidation in a counterintuitive way. For a DCA portfolio built over 12 years at 8% returns, the oldest 25% of units carry approximately 1.3× the average gain ratio, while the newest 25% carry only 0.7× the average. **Selling the first 25% of units under FIFO therefore realizes approximately 32.5% of total embedded gains**, not 25%. Selling half the portfolio realizes roughly 60% of total gains. This FIFO gradient means you cannot escape the tax as efficiently as a proportional model would suggest — but it also means that what you *retain* after partial liquidation carries below-average embedded gains, reducing your deferred tax liability.

|-----------|-----------|--------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| €180K | €108,000 | €23,720 | €19,373 | €13,784 | €7,251 |
| €220K | €132,000 | €29,240 | €23,927 | €17,096 | €8,889 |
| €280K | €168,000 | €37,520 | €30,758 | €22,064 | €11,438 |
| €350K | €210,000 | €47,580 | €38,728 | €27,860 | €14,578 |

The marginal tax savings diminish as you reduce liquidation further. Cutting from 100% to 50% saves €9,936–€19,720 depending on portfolio size, while cutting from 50% to 25% saves only €6,533–€13,282. The first reduction matters most because it removes gains from the top of the progressive scale.

## Mortgage costs are lower than most people assume

The Spanish fixed mortgage market in March 2026 offers historically attractive rates. The best bonified 15-year fixed rates sit around **2.10–2.40% TIN** from banks like Unicaja, Ibercaja, and Openbank, with brokers negotiating as low as 2.10% for strong borrower profiles. Clean rates (no bonification requirements) run 2.40–2.85%. A borrower with a large portfolio, stable income, and low LTV (20–50%) occupies an unusually strong negotiating position.

Since the 2019 mortgage law (Ley 5/2019) and the 2018 AJD reform, **the bank pays virtually all mortgage formalization costs**: notary, land registry, AJD stamp duty (1.5% in Valencia, dropping to 1.4% from June 2026), and gestoría. The buyer's incremental costs for taking a mortgage are modest — a tasación (€300–500) and annual life insurance for bonification (€250–400/year). Home insurance is needed regardless of whether you take a mortgage.

Early repayment provisions are borrower-friendly. Fixed-rate mortgages cap penalties at **2% of capital repaid in the first 10 years and 1.5% thereafter** — but only if the bank suffers actual financial loss from the prepayment. In a stable or rising rate environment, the bank has no financial loss and therefore cannot charge any penalty. BBVA and MyInvestor explicitly charge zero early repayment fees. For an investor planning aggressive repayment at €1,500/month against a €700–800 required payment, total penalty costs over the loan's life are estimated at €800–1,200 maximum.

The total interest and ancillary cost picture for a €200K property with accelerated repayment at €1,500/month:

| Mortgage (15yr term) | Rate 2.5% | Rate 3.5% | Rate 4.5% |
|---------------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| €60K — payoff 3.6yr, total interest | €2,800 | €3,900 | €5,100 |
| €80K — payoff 4.8yr, total interest | €5,100 | €7,000 | €9,100 |
| €100K — payoff 6.2yr, total interest | €8,200 | €11,300 | €14,700 |
| Life insurance (over accel. term) | €700–1,900 | €700–1,900 | €700–1,900 |
| Tasación + fees | €600–1,000 | €600–1,000 | €600–1,000 |

With accelerated repayment, **a €100K mortgage at 3.5% costs approximately €13,000–14,000 in total** (interest + insurance + fees), paid off in 6.2 years. This is the cost to compare against the CGT saved by not liquidating that €100K of portfolio.

## Retained compounding creates a decisive advantage

The mortgage strategy works because it simultaneously generates two benefits: an immediate CGT saving (a lump sum) and a compounding advantage (the retained portfolio grows from a higher starting base). The comparison is not simply "mortgage rate vs portfolio return" — the CGT saving acts as a free head start.

Consider the base case: €280K portfolio, 60% embedded gains, €200K property plus €20K acquisition costs. With a **50% LTV mortgage (€100K)**, you sell €120K from the portfolio (retaining €160K) and pay approximately **€19,000 in CGT**. Without a mortgage, you sell €220K (retaining €60K) and pay approximately **€33,000 in CGT**. The CGT saving of **€14,000** is immediately available for compounding alongside the €100K additional retained portfolio.

The net financial comparison over 15 years works as follows. Strategy A (mortgage) starts with roughly €160K invested minus €19K CGT = **€141K net portfolio**, pays the mortgage aggressively from the €1,500/month capacity, and begins investing the full €1,500/month after payoff (~month 74). Strategy B (no mortgage) starts with €60K minus deferred adjustments ≈ **€27K net portfolio** but invests the full €1,500/month from day one. The critical dynamic: Strategy A's €114K head start in invested capital compounds for the full 15 years, while Strategy B slowly builds up fresh capital from zero.

At 7% annualized returns and 3.5% mortgage rate:

| LTV | Mortgage | CGT saved | 15yr wealth advantage |
|-----|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 20% | €40K | ~€5,000 | +€16,000 |
| 30% | €60K | ~€8,000 | +€28,000 |
| 40% | €80K | ~€11,000 | +€42,000 |
| 50% | €100K | ~€14,000 | +€59,000 |

The advantage scales roughly linearly with LTV — each additional €20K of mortgage generates approximately €13,000–17,000 of additional 15-year wealth. This linearity holds because the CGT brackets stay in the 21–23% range for these gain amounts, and the compounding math is proportional.

**Breakeven portfolio returns are extraordinarily low.** Even at 50% LTV with a 4.5% mortgage rate, the portfolio only needs to return **~1.9% annualized** for the mortgage strategy to break even — a figure below even money market yields. The breakeven is so low because the CGT saving (~€14,000 on €100K retained) represents an immediate ~14% return on the capital retained. At 2.5% mortgage rates, breakeven drops to 0.8%. With 60% embedded gains, the CGT shield is so powerful that the mortgage dominates across virtually all realistic market scenarios.

When embedded gains are lower, the breakeven rises but remains manageable: at 20% gains, breakeven is approximately 3.0% (50% LTV, 3.5% rate); at 40% gains, it drops to 2.2%. **The mortgage strategy only fails when embedded gains are under ~10% AND expected returns are very low AND mortgage rates exceed 5%** — a scenario that contradicts the premise of a long-held DCA portfolio.

## Phased liquidation saves less than you might expect

The alternative to a mortgage — liquidating the portfolio across multiple tax years by traspaso-ing to a money market fund and redeeming in annual tranches — provides mathematically modest savings for portfolios under €350K. The mechanism works because each calendar year's gains are taxed independently in fresh progressive brackets, effectively reusing the 19% and 21% bands.

The confirmed traspaso rules make this operationally feasible: transferring from the Fidelity MSCI World fund (IE00BYX5NX33, CNMV registration 1709, confirmed traspaso-eligible) to a money market fund is **tax-free**, with the new units inheriting the original cost basis. The money market fund can then be redeemed in annual tranches without the investor ever holding volatile equity exposure during the phasing period.

However, the **maximum tax saving from bracket reuse is €1,120 per additional year** when all gains fall within the €50K–€200K range. This is the fixed benefit of reusing the 19% bracket (€240 saved) and 21% bracket (€880 saved) one additional time. For a €280K portfolio with 60% gains (€168K total), phasing over 4 years saves approximately **€2,720 compared to single-year liquidation** — meaningful but far smaller than the €14,000+ saved by taking a mortgage.

| Portfolio | Total gain | 1-year tax | 4-year tax | Saving | Saving % |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|--------|----------|
| €180K, 60% | €108,000 | €23,720 | €22,200 | €1,520 | 6.4% |
| €220K, 60% | €132,000 | €29,240 | €27,240 | €2,000 | 6.8% |
| €280K, 60% | €168,000 | €37,520 | €34,800 | €2,720 | 7.3% |
| €350K, 70% | €245,000 | €57,030 | €51,870 | €5,160 | 9.0% |

The savings jump meaningfully only when gains cross the **€200,000 threshold** — the 23%→27% bracket boundary. For the €350K portfolio at 70% gains (€245K total gain), the first-to-second-year split alone saves €2,920 by keeping some gains below €200K. This is the only scenario in the analyzed range where phased liquidation provides more than marginal benefit.

Practical constraints limit the phased approach. You need the full purchase amount on notary day. The cleanest execution is a **December/January split**: traspaso equity to money market, redeem the first tranche in late December (taxed in the current year), redeem the second tranche in early January (taxed in the following year), then purchase the property in January or February. This spreads gains across two tax years with minimal delay — perhaps 6–8 weeks — and saves one year's worth of bracket reuse (~€1,120 for gains under €200K). Phasing over 3–4 years requires delaying the purchase by 1–3 years, introducing property price risk, continued rental costs, and opportunity costs that likely exceed the tax savings.

**The combined approach — partial mortgage plus December/January split on the liquidated portion — captures both optimizations.** Taking a 40–50% mortgage reduces the amount to liquidate, and splitting that smaller amount across two tax years provides a further €1,000–1,200 saving. This combination is strictly superior to either strategy alone and adds minimal complexity.

## Total cost optimization across the parameter space

Combining all three variables — CGT saved, mortgage interest paid, and retained compounding — into a single wealth-at-year-15 metric reveals a robust pattern. The table below shows total financial wealth (excluding the property, which is identical across strategies) for the base case (€280K portfolio, 60% gains, €200K property, €20K acquisition costs, accelerated repayment at €1,500/month):

| | 5% return | 7% return | 9% return |
|---|---|---|---|
| **0% LTV (full liquidation)** | ~€503K | ~€607K | ~€757K |
| **Mortgage 2.5%** | | | |
| 30% LTV | +€19K | +€31K | +€47K |
| 50% LTV | +€36K | +€62K | +€101K |
| **Mortgage 3.5%** | | | |
| 30% LTV | +€17K | +€28K | +€44K |
| 50% LTV | +€33K | +€59K | +€96K |
| **Mortgage 4.5%** | | | |
| 30% LTV | +€14K | +€25K | +€40K |
| 50% LTV | +€26K | +€53K | +€89K |

Several patterns are immediately apparent. **Higher LTV is always better** within the 0–50% range tested — every additional euro of mortgage saves CGT and compounds, and the marginal benefit per unit of LTV does not diminish within this range. **The return assumption matters far more than the mortgage rate**: the spread between 5% and 9% returns at 50% LTV dwarfs the spread between 2.5% and 4.5% mortgage rates at any return level. And **even the most conservative scenario** (5% returns, 4.5% rate, 30% LTV) still generates a €14,000 advantage over full liquidation.

Sensitivity to embedded gain ratio is also important. At 40% gains, the CGT saved per €100K retained drops from ~€14,000 to ~€9,000, reducing but not eliminating the mortgage advantage. At 70% gains, it rises to ~€18,000, making the case even stronger. The interaction with portfolio size matters mainly through the FIFO effect: larger portfolios with higher gains push more euros into higher brackets, making the CGT savings from mortgage proportionally larger.

The analysis reveals that the optimization landscape has **no interior optimum** within realistic parameters. The "right" LTV is not some specific balanced point like 30% — it is simply the highest LTV the investor is comfortable with, constrained by practical factors: bank willingness to lend at low property values, comfort with debt, and cash flow management during the repayment period.

## A practical decision rule

The quantitative analysis supports a simple three-part heuristic:

**If your embedded gain ratio exceeds 30% and the available fixed mortgage rate is below 4.5%, take the maximum comfortable LTV mortgage (targeting 40–50%), repay aggressively with your full monthly capacity, and keep the retained portfolio invested.** The expected wealth advantage over 15 years is €25,000–€100,000 depending on returns and LTV, with a breakeven portfolio return below 3%.

**If your total realized gains would exceed €200,000 in a single tax year, phase the liquidation across at least two calendar years** using the December/January traspaso-to-money-market technique. This avoids the 27% bracket and saves €1,500–€5,000 depending on how far above €200K the gains extend. For gains under €200K, phasing saves only ~€1,120 per extra year and may not justify purchase delays.

**If the above gain threshold is not crossed and you have a mortgage, a December/January split on the liquidated portion still saves ~€1,120 with minimal effort.** This is worth doing almost always since it requires only timing, not a delay in purchase.

In simplified form: for every €100K of embedded gains above 30%, taking a mortgage of approximately €80K–€100K and repaying it over 5–7 years (with €1,500/month capacity) produces roughly **€40,000–€60,000 of additional 15-year wealth at 7% returns**, net of all mortgage costs. The mortgage effectively costs ~€1,500–2,000 net (after CGT savings offset the interest), while generating 25–40× that amount in compounding benefit.

## Spanish-specific mechanics that shape the strategy

**No mortgage interest deduction exists for post-2013 purchases.** The deducción por inversión en vivienda habitual was eliminated by Real Decreto-ley 20/2011 for purchases from 1 January 2013 onward. A transitional regime exists only for pre-2013 buyers who applied the deduction in 2012 or earlier. No new legislation has been proposed to restore it. This means mortgage interest is a pure cost with zero tax offset.

**Comunitat Valenciana regional deductions offer nothing relevant** to this investor. The only property purchase deduction (5% of amounts paid, excluding interest) requires the buyer to be aged 35 or under with income below €30,000 — inapplicable here. A marginal mortgage interest increase deduction (50% of year-over-year interest increase, capped at €100) is economically trivial. The region offers no deductions for capital gains, investment income, or fund redemptions.

**ITP in Comunitat Valenciana drops from 10% to 9% on 1 June 2026** under Ley 5/2025. For a €200K property, this saves €2,000 — a meaningful incentive to time the purchase after that date. AJD also drops from 1.5% to 1.4%, though this falls on the bank for mortgage deeds. Properties above €1M face an 11% rate.

**The IVF guarantee programme is likely inapplicable.** It requires the buyer to be under 45 years old, a first-time homeowner, and a continuous Valencia resident for 2 years prior to application. The programme provides a free guarantee enabling up to 100% LTV with participating banks (CaixaBank, Santander, Sabadell, Cajamar, and several local cajas). While the maximum property value of €311,000 and the programme's terms are generous, the age restriction effectively eliminates it for a buyer aged 48–55.

**Portfolio-backed lending (pignoración) offers a powerful alternative** for avoiding CGT entirely. MyInvestor provides the most accessible product: loans secured against investment funds at **3.25% TIN for LTV ≤50%** of portfolio value, with 20-year maximum terms and zero commissions. For a €280K portfolio, this means borrowing up to €140K — enough to cover much of a €200K purchase when combined with a small liquidation. However, pignoración carries **margin call risk**: a ~30% portfolio decline triggers a collateral call, and a ~40% decline allows forced liquidation. The rate premium over a regular mortgage (3.25% vs 2.1–2.4%) also adds cost. A regular mortgage secured against the property carries no margin call risk and offers lower rates, making it generally preferable unless the investor specifically wants to avoid any portfolio liquidation. Major banks (CaixaBank, BBVA, Santander) also offer pignoración through private banking divisions at negotiable rates, typically for larger portfolios.

**The multi-ISIN FIFO optimization** deserves mention as a long-term planning tool. Since FIFO applies per-ISIN, investors can hold multiple funds tracking the same index (e.g., Fidelity, Vanguard, and Amundi MSCI World funds). At liquidation, they can choose which ISIN to redeem first — selecting the one with the lowest embedded gains per unit. This gives partial control over which "vintage" of capital gains is realized. While it does not help for an already-accumulated single-ISIN portfolio, future DCA contributions could be directed to a different ISIN to create this optionality.

## Conclusion: the architecture of an optimal purchase

The analysis reveals a hierarchy of optimization levers ranked by impact. The partial mortgage is the dominant lever, saving **€25,000–€100,000 over 15 years** through the interaction of CGT avoidance and compound growth. Phased liquidation is a secondary lever worth **€1,000–€5,000**, implementable with minimal friction via a December/January redemption split. The ITP timing (purchasing after June 2026 to capture the 10%→9% reduction) saves a further **€1,500–€2,500**. Together, these three levers combined can preserve **€28,000–€108,000** of wealth compared to a naïve full-liquidation-and-pay-cash approach.

The surprising finding is that the optimal LTV has no interior maximum — more mortgage is always better within the 0–50% range, even at elevated rates. This challenges the conventional instinct to minimize debt. The reason is structural: Spanish CGT brackets are progressive while mortgage interest is flat, and the CGT saving acts as an immediate return on retained capital that exceeds mortgage interest costs at any plausible portfolio return.

The practical implementation for the base case (€280K portfolio, 60% gains, €200K property, purchase after June 2026) would be: traspaso the full equity fund to the money market fund in Q3 of the purchase year; redeem the first tranche in December (enough for the expected down payment plus costs minus mortgage); redeem the second tranche in January; take a **40–50% LTV 15-year fixed mortgage at the best available bonified rate** (targeting ≤2.5%); direct the full €1,500/month to mortgage payoff, clearing the debt in 5–7 years; then redirect the full capacity to rebuilding the portfolio for retirement. The retained portfolio can remain in the money market fund during the transition or be traspaso'd back into equities once the purchase is complete, all without triggering any additional tax.
