## Research focus

### 1. Historical precedent: geopolitically-triggered drawdowns

How long do equity drawdowns last when triggered by Middle East conflicts or energy price shocks? Examine:
- Gulf War (1990–91), Iraq War (2003), Arab Spring (2011), Iran tensions (2019–20), Russia-Ukraine (2022)
- For each: how deep was the initial drawdown, how long to trough, how long to recovery?
- Were there cases where the "obvious" crisis turned into a multi-year bear market, and what distinguished those from
  quick recoveries?
- Specifically: does a >30% gas price spike in <1 month have historical precedent, and what happened to equities in the
  12 months following?

### 2. Energy price transmission into equity markets

The gas price spike is >30% in under a month. How does this propagate?
- What sectors and regions are most exposed? (Europe is a heavy gas importer — does the 25% Europe allocation create
  outsized risk?)
- How quickly do energy price shocks transmit into corporate earnings and equity valuations?
- Is there a threshold (e.g., sustained >$X oil or >€Y gas) beyond which the economic damage becomes self-reinforcing
  (recession trigger)?
- Current energy prices vs those thresholds — where are we?

### 3. The 55/25/20 allocation's specific exposure

Does the geographic tilt toward Europe and EM create additional vulnerability to this specific crisis compared to a
plain MSCI World portfolio?
- Europe (25% of portfolio): energy dependence, proximity to conflict, impact on the "valuation normalization" thesis
  that justifies the overweight
- EM (20%): oil-exporting EM vs oil-importing EM within the MSCI EM index — is the net effect positive, negative, or
  neutral?
- MSCI World (55%): the US portion (~70% of this sleeve) — is the US relatively insulated or also affected?

### 4. Staging analysis: cost of waiting 4–8 weeks

If the crisis resolves or stabilizes within 1–2 months (the most common outcome for geopolitical shocks):
- What is the expected cost of deploying €7K in 4 weekly tranches of €1,750 vs immediately?
- What is the expected cost of waiting 8 weeks then deploying in full?
- How do these costs change if the drawdown deepens to -10%, -20%, -30% before recovery?
- At what point does the "buy the dip" opportunity from waiting outweigh the time-out-of-market cost?

### 5. Decision framework

Given the findings above, provide a concrete decision rule:
- Under what conditions should I deploy immediately (this week)?
- Under what conditions should I stage over 4–8 weeks?
- Under what conditions should I hold in cash and wait for a specific signal?
- What signals should I monitor over the next 1–4 weeks to update the decision?
