# Communication Coaching Rubric

Loaded by the personal assistant during coaching tasks. Not needed for awareness or drafting.

## Evaluation Dimensions

Score each 1-5:

1. **Uncertainty reduction** — Does the draft localize uncertainty to the actual unknown, or
   does it spread vagueness across the whole message?
2. **Response friction** — How easy is it for the recipient to take the next step? Lower is
   better: one clear ask > multiple ambiguous asks.
3. **Composure** — Does the draft maintain poise under pressure, or does it leak anxiety,
   over-explain, or apologize unnecessarily?
4. **Warmth-competence balance** — Is warmth present but not undermining competence? Warmth
   that costs nothing in clarity stays. Warmth that lowers authority goes.
5. **Authority growth** — Does the draft increase or decrease the sender's perceived authority,
   ownership, and leadership over time?

## Collapse Risk

Assess: `Low`, `Medium`, or `High`

Collapse risk is the probability that the draft triggers a negative spiral — defensiveness,
escalation, loss of trust, or perceived incompetence. High collapse risk means the draft
contains a structural weakness that could backfire under pressure.

## Authority Mechanisms

Identify which mechanism the draft uses or should use:

- **Clarity anchor** — The draft eliminates ambiguity about what happened, what's needed, or
  what the state is. Authority comes from being the person who makes things legible.
- **Bounded ask** — The draft makes a specific, scoped request rather than an open-ended one.
  Easier to say yes to, harder to deflect.
- **Ownership signal** — The draft names who owns what and when. "I'll handle X by Friday"
  is an ownership signal.
- **Decision framing** — The draft presents a decision with clear options and a recommendation,
  rather than dumping an open question.
- **Formulation** — The draft names a pattern, category, or frame that organizes a messy
  situation. "This is a prioritization question, not a technical one."
- **Calibrated pushback** — The draft disagrees or redirects without escalating. It names the
  concern, explains why, and proposes an alternative.
- **Strategic visibility** — The draft makes work visible to the right people at the right
  time. Not self-promotion — legibility of contribution.
- **Trust-preserving warmth** — The draft is warm in a way that builds trust without
  undermining competence. "Happy to help" when genuine and low-cost.
- **Authority maintenance** — The draft holds the line on a previous decision or commitment
  without re-litigating it. "We agreed to X — I'm proceeding unless I hear otherwise."

## Tightening Principles

When rewriting:

- Preserve intent — change execution, not meaning
- Reduce friction — fewer steps for the recipient to take
- Localize uncertainty — scope hedging to the exact unknown, not the whole message
- Remove unnecessary apology — if repair isn't needed, apology lowers authority
- Remove self-positioning — "I generally try to..." adds nothing the work state doesn't show
- Make ownership explicit — if the sender owns something, name it
- Make the next step explicit — if there's a clear next move, state it

## Calibration Notes

- If the draft is already good, say so. Don't force changes for the sake of producing output.
- If the user asks for a stronger pass, tighten more aggressively but keep intent.
- If the user asks for softer wording, preserve the authority mechanism while reducing edge.
- Robotic bluntness is not authority — it is social clumsiness.
- Adding explanation to feel safer often increases friction instead of safety.
